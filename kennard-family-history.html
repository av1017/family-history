Origins — Scotland to America

The Kennard name traces back to Norman France, arriving in England during the Norman Conquest of 1066 as "Kenewardus" — recorded in the Domesday Book of 1086. Some of these Norman settlers made their way to Scotland, near Aberdeen. According to family lore, an ancestor supported the Scottish "Pretender" in a bid to free Scotland from England. When that effort failed, he lost all his land and fled — first to northern Scotland, then to the Ulster region of Ireland.

Upon immigrating to America, the family simplified "Kinnaird" to "Kennard." An old Scottish schoolmaster scolded them for abandoning the original spelling. In response, three brothers went three different ways: one returned to "Kinnaird," one compromised with "Kinnard," and one kept "Kennard." Your line descends from the "Kennard" brother.

The earliest documented American ancestor is Richard Kennard, who married Mary Pullen in 1693 in Kent County, Maryland, where they owned a 700-acre plantation called "Danby." From Richard and Mary, the family line runs unbroken through four Maryland generations before beginning the long southwestern migration that would eventually land them in Texas.

The Long March Southwest

Over roughly 40 years, your ancestors moved steadily southwest in search of cheap, fertile land — a common ambition of the era.

Michael Kennard (your 5th great-grandfather) moves his family from Maryland to Brunswick County, North Carolina. His brother Nathaniel makes the same move.

The family moves again, settling near Murfreesboro in what would become Rutherford County, Tennessee — just as Tennessee became a state in 1796.

Anthony Drew and his brother Michael II move their families to the Alabama Territory (near Greensboro). Anthony Drew was even proposed for Colonel of Shelby County, though the appointment didn't happen.

Anthony Drew and Sarah sign on with Empresario Sterling Robertson's colony to move to Tejas (Mexico). They reach the Sabine River border in 1830 — only to be blocked by Mexico's Decree of April 6, 1830, which nearly halted all Anglo immigration. They wait.
December 24, 1832
Christmas Eve in Texas 🎄
Anthony Drew is granted ~4,400 acres in what will become Grimes County. The family arrives at their land on Christmas Eve. Their first night: they try to hunt wild turkeys for a Christmas feast. The sons return empty-handed. They cook their last old rooster instead.

Life in Early Texas

The Kennards settled on land that was then part of the Mexican state of Coahuila. They were original colonists — among the very first Anglo-American families to put down permanent roots in what would become Grimes County. Their land near present-day Anderson was long known as "Kennards' Prairie" — later renamed Roans Prairie, which still exists today along Texas State Highway 90.

🛤️ The Origin of Three Notch Road

One of Anthony Drew's sons was sent on horseback to a trading post in Madison County (15–20 miles away). On the return trip, he "gave the horse her head" — letting the mare find her own way home, trusting her instinct to return to her foal. The mare took a shorter route nobody knew about, passing through what are now Bedias and Singleton, Texas, where the boy discovered a freshwater spring. The next morning, Anthony Drew followed his son's muddy hoof prints and blazed the route by cutting three notches in trees with a hand axe. The trail became a road used by settlers and Indians alike. Today it is Texas FM 2562, still locally called "Three Notch Road." Anthony Drew and Sarah are buried beside it, where the State of Texas erected a monument in their honor in 1936.

🏨 The Fanthorpe Inn

Michael Moore Kennard (your 3rd great-grandfather) went into business with his father-in-law Abraham Womack and his brother-in-law Henry Fanthorpe — an English immigrant who had married Michael's sister, Rachael. Their store, "Fanthorpe, Womack, and Kennard," was the first mercantile in what is now Anderson, Texas. Fanthorpe also built an inn at the crossroads of two stagecoach lines. The Fanthorpe Inn became one of the most famous hotels in the Republic of Texas. Its guest list reads like a history textbook: Sam Houston, Zachary Taylor, Robert E. Lee, Stonewall Jackson, Jefferson Davis, and Ulysses S. Grant all stayed there. The town of Anderson was named after Kenneth Lewis Anderson, Vice President of the Republic of Texas — who died at the inn and is buried across the street from it.

The Texas Revolution (1835–1836)

When tensions with Mexico boiled over into war, the Kennards were in the middle of it. Anthony Drew and four of his sons — Michael Moore, William Everett, Anthony Drew Jr., and Marquis de Lafayette — all served in the Texas Army.

📰 The Night the Alamo News Came

The Kennard family learned of the fall of the Alamo from a houseguest: Dr. Anson Jones, a signer of Texas' Declaration of Independence and the future last President of the Republic of Texas. He spent the night at the Kennard home on his way through the area and delivered the devastating news himself. Every Anglo defender of the Alamo had been killed.

William Everett Kennard is reported to have fought at the decisive Battle of San Jacinto on April 21, 1836, where Texas won its independence. His name appears on the San Jacinto Monument near Houston. Michael Moore Kennard (your 3rd great-grandfather) was reportedly on baggage duty and was ill the day of the battle. After independence, the soldiers were paid in land grants — adding significantly to the Kennard family's already substantial holdings.

"Captain William Everett Kennard was a noble man worthy to be remembered and a truer friend I have never had." — Abraham Zuber, fellow San Jacinto campaign veteran

The Republic of Texas existed as an independent nation for over nine years (1836–1845), before being annexed by the United States on December 29, 1845.

Family Stories & Lore

🪶 The Indian Raid — Mrs. Taylor

In 1840, Indians raided the area. Some neighbors took refuge in the Hadley home next to the Kennards; others barricaded in the Kennard house. A woman named Mrs. Taylor, whose husband was at the Kennard house, left the safety of the Hadley home with her children to join him — against everyone's pleas. The Indians overtook her. She was killed, her children taken captive — in full view of those watching from the Hadley house. The Kennard men pursued the Indians and eventually rescued the children, bringing them to Rachael Kennard Fanthorpe's home for safety.

🙏 "Dat Am a Mighty Pore Help"

On another occasion, when all the men were away from the Kennard home, an old enslaved woman discovered Indians killing the family's calves nearby. She rushed to tell Sarah Moore Kennard (your 4th great-grandmother). When Sarah made no move to act, the woman cried, "Missi what shall we do? What shall we do?" Sarah replied calmly, "We can do nothing but trust in the Lord." The woman shot back: "Dat am a mighty pore help when de Injuns am out dar killin de calves."

💛 The Yellow Fever Vigil

During a yellow fever epidemic in Anderson, a stagecoach driver died of the disease at the Fanthorpe Inn. Rachael Kennard Fanthorpe (sister-in-law of Mary Ann Womack Kennard) had nursed him without knowing what he had, and she too fell ill. The entire community fled in fear, and a doctor quarantined Rachael. For six weeks, Mary Ann Womack Kennard (your 3rd great-grandmother) stayed and nursed her — burning or boiling her clothes each day, bathing in "strongly medicated water." She believed she was risking her life. She did it anyway. Rachael died. Mary Ann did not contract the disease. It was only later discovered that yellow fever spreads by mosquitoes, not human contact — but that knowledge didn't exist at the time. Her bravery stands regardless.

🔫 Standing Guard Over a Grave

After the Civil War, during Reconstruction, Union troops passed by Michael Moore Kennard's country home. A Confederate soldier — likely Mary Ann's brother Volney Womack, killed at the Battle of Pleasant Hill, Louisiana in 1864 — had been temporarily buried in their front yard wrapped in charcoal. The soldiers suspected the mound held hidden meat and threatened to dig it up. Michael Moore sat on his porch with two guns across his lap and told them he would kill any man who went near the grave. After a heated standoff, the soldiers saw he meant it — and left. (Had they looked in the nearby creek bottom woods, they would have found large quantities of meat being guarded by the family's former slaves.)

🐎 The Riderless Horse

During the Civil War, Anthony Drew Kennard (a son of Michael Moore, named for his grandfather) died of pneumonia in Richmond, Virginia on November 27, 1861. The family learned of his death in the most gut-wrenching way: from their front porch, they watched a distant figure approaching down the lane. Slowly, they made out the shape of his enslaved servant leading Anthony Drew's horse — riderless. The family pieced together the awful truth before a single word was spoken.

🤝 Saved by a Masonic Sign

A Kennard ancestor fighting for the Confederacy was captured by Union forces and placed in a firing line for immediate execution. Just before the order was given, he made a secret Masonic hand sign. The Union officer commanding the execution squad was also a Mason. He pulled the Kennard ancestor from the line. Everyone else in the line was shot and killed.

The Civil War

Texas voted to secede from the Union on February 23, 1861. Multiple Kennard men served in the Confederate military. The most decorated was Captain John R. Kennard, youngest son of Anthony Drew and Sarah — making him your 4th great-grand-uncle.

John R. organized the "Grimes Boys" infantry company, which became part of the 10th Texas Infantry. He was elected Captain by his own men. At the Battle of Missionary Ridge (Chattanooga, November 1863), his brigade commander and colonel were both wounded in the first charge, and John R. was placed in command of the combined 6th, 10th, and 15th Consolidated Texas Infantry — thousands of men. He handled the command with skill, including at the subsequent Battle of Ringgold Gap. After the war, he became a State District Judge in Anderson, Texas.

Papa & Mama Kennard — Your 2nd Great-Grandparents

John H. Kennard (b. 1852) and Hattie Brown Kennard (b. 1856) were the heart of the Grimes County Kennard family in their era. Known affectionately to their grandchildren as "Papa Kennard" and "Mama Kennard," they had six children.

The First National Bank of Anderson

Papa Kennard's greatest achievement was founding the First National Bank of Anderson, which opened June 27, 1904. The bank employed and supported Kennard descendants for at least four generations — including your great-great-grandfather James Gary Thomas Sr. as Vice President, and even Richard Gary Thomas (the author) who borrowed money from it to pay for law school. The bank still operates today as one of Grimes County's most prominent financial institutions.

Tragedy: Hiram Kennard

One of Papa and Mama Kennard's sons, Hiram, was accidentally shot and killed by his own brother George Brown on December 20, 1894 — five days before Christmas — at age 19. George Brown's revolver slipped from his pocket as they sat down to rest on their walk home, struck a rock, and discharged. The family rarely spoke of it.

Stella Kennard Thomas — Your Great-Grandmother

Stella Scott Kennard (born April 11, 1882) was a daughter of John H. and Hattie Brown Kennard. She married James Gary Thomas Sr. on June 18, 1907. Richard Gary Thomas (the author) called her "Grandmama" and wrote about her with more warmth than almost anyone else in the entire document.

Stella was a study in contradictions — simultaneously Victorian and fearless:

The Scholar

Graduated Kidd-Key College in Sherman, TX. Was a Latin scholar who read books in Latin for pleasure. Tutored high school students in algebra and plane geometry.

The Musician

An accomplished pianist with a Steinway grand. Played for her church, for family gatherings, and for herself. Neighbors two blocks away could hear her through open windows.

The Victorian

Never entered a grocery store. Never drove a car. Refused to say "arm" or "leg" — only "limb." Banned alcohol and playing cards from her home. Never judged those who indulged.

The Rancher

Ran a cattle operation of 1,800+ acres with her sons. In her mid-to-late 70s, she walked among 300–400 semi-wild cattle in a crowded pen, carrying a walking stick, inspecting every head. Her sons begged her to stop. She didn't.

"To say that she was most meaningful in my life is frankly a gross understatement. I loved her very much. I still do." — Richard Gary Thomas, writing about Stella in 2018

Stella had three sons: Kennard Thomas (b. 1908), Marion Brown Thomas (b. 1913), and J.G. Thomas Sr. (b. 1924) — your great-grandfather. All three were college graduates, which was notable for Grimes County in that era.
