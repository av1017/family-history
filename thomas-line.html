Origins — Colonial Virginia

The Thomas line is one of the oldest traceable lines in this family, rooted in colonial Virginia before the American nation existed. The earliest confirmed ancestor, Joseph Thomas Sr., was born around 1680 in Nansemond County, Virginia — a county on the south shore of Hampton Roads, settled by English colonists in the 1630s. He married Alice Spivey, born around 1687 in Lancashire, England, suggesting she was either an immigrant herself or the daughter of immigrants from northern England.

Over the following 150 years, the Thomas family moved steadily south and west — the classic pattern of English colonial families following the agricultural frontier: from Virginia to North Carolina, then Georgia, then Alabama, and finally into Texas. The full chain from Joseph Thomas to Avery spans ten generations and over 300 years.

Nansemond County, Virginia — Where It Begins

Nansemond County was one of the original Virginia counties, established in 1637. It sits on the south bank of Hampton Roads, across from Norfolk. English settlement of the area began in the 1620s–1630s. Joseph Thomas being born there around 1680 places him squarely in the third generation of Virginia colonists — his grandparents likely came directly from England. Alice Spivey's Lancashire origins suggest a northwest England connection; Lancashire was a heavily Protestant-contested county, and many Lancashire families emigrated to Virginia in the 17th century.

The Migration — Virginia to Texas

~1680
Virginia — The Beginning
Joseph Thomas Sr. born in Nansemond County, Virginia. Alice Spivey born in Lancashire, England. They represent the earliest confirmed generation of the Thomas family in America.
1714
James Clark Thomas — Revolutionary War Patriot
Born in Nansemond County, Virginia. Served in the Continental forces from 19 December 1775 through 1783. Died November 1780 in Bertie County, North Carolina. His service makes Avery potentially eligible for Sons of the American Revolution membership.
~1800
Georgia — John Thomas & Phoebe Ghiselin
John Thomas (b. ~1782, South Carolina) marries Phoebe Ghiselin on 26 June 1801 in Warren County, Georgia — confirmed by Louis Poole's primary-source research from the original Warren County marriage record. Note: later family records sometimes show "Phebe Springer," a confusion with neighboring families on Austin's Colony Register; Poole's document is definitive. Pre-1782 Virginia lineage (Joseph ~1680 → James ~1714 → Joseph ~1740) is family tradition, not yet confirmed by primary sources.
1812
James Avery Thomas — Born in Alabama
Born 31 October 1812 in Marengo County, Alabama. The family has followed the Deep South frontier west through the Carolinas and Georgia into Alabama. James Avery would become Avery's namesake.
December 1832
Austin's Colony — Texas
John Thomas and Phoebe Ghiselin register with Stephen F. Austin's Colony in December 1832 — confirmed by Austin's Register of Families (primary source). Their son James Avery Thomas arrives in Texas with the family as part of the first wave of American settlers into Mexican-controlled Texas.
1852
Francis Marion Thomas — Born in Texas
Born 9 August 1852 in Bedias, Grimes County, Texas — the first Thomas generation born as a Texan. Grimes County is in east-central Texas, the first region settled by Americans from Louisiana and Alabama in the 1820s–1840s.
1877–1924
Three Generations in Grimes County
James Gary Thomas Sr. (b. 1877), then J.G. Thomas Jr. (b. 1924) — both born in Anderson, Grimes County, Texas. The family is now firmly rooted in central Texas.
1960–1998
Houston — Michael Scott Thomas & Avery
Michael Scott Thomas (b. 1960, Harris County, TX). Avery Thomas (b. 1998, Texas). Ten generations after Joseph Thomas in colonial Virginia, the line continues.

Stories & Context

James Avery Thomas — Avery's Namesake

James Avery Thomas (b. 31 Oct 1812, Marengo Co., Alabama · d. 28 Apr 1865, Grimes Co., TX) is Avery's 4th great-grandfather and the source of the name "Avery" carried forward through generations. He arrived in Texas in December 1832 with his parents as part of the first wave of American settlers. He married Amanda K. Wheeler on 28 April 1842 and died in Grimes County in 1865, buried at Mustang Cemetery.

James Clark Thomas — Revolutionary War Service

James Clark Thomas (b. 1714, Nansemond Co., VA · d. Nov 1780, Bertie Co., NC) served in the Continental forces beginning 19 December 1775 — just months after Lexington and Concord. His confirmed service through 1783 makes him an SAR qualifying patriot ancestor. His descendant Michael Scott Thomas is already a member of the Sons of the Republic of Texas; the SAR application through the James Clark Thomas line is a documented open research goal.

Austin's Colony, December 1832 — Among the First Texans

John Thomas and Phoebe Ghiselin registered with Stephen F. Austin's Colony in December 1832 — confirmed by Austin's Register of Families, a primary source. This is earlier than the "1835" date that appeared in some family records (the 1835 date is not supported by the Register). Austin's colonists were required to be Catholics or willing to convert, to be of good character, and to commit to farming the land. The original register entry for the Thomas family is a primary source held in this archive.

The Thomas & Kennard Lines — United in Marriage

The Thomas and Kennard families both ran through Marengo County, Alabama and Grimes County, Texas in overlapping eras — likely moving in the same social circles through the antebellum South before their descendants married. The union of James Gary Thomas Sr. and Stella Kennard joined the two lines permanently. Stella was the daughter of John H. "Papa" Kennard, the central figure of Richard Gary Thomas's 93-page Kennard Family History.
