Companion to: Thomas Family History (B5 C19) — the two chapters cross-reference extensively

This chapter is far more speculative than the Thomas chapter — Poole is candid that many courthouse records for the Wheeler family's path through Georgia and Alabama were destroyed during the Civil War. He distinguishes carefully between what is documented and what is "circumstantial." The Amanda Wheeler–James Avery Thomas connection, however, is firmly established.

How the Wheeler Family Connects to the Thomas Family

Core Connection

Amanda Wheeler (b. 15 May 1827, Georgia; d. 5 Sep 1896) married James Avery Thomas on 28 April 1842 in Montgomery County, Texas. She was the daughter of Henry H. Wheeler and Mary Ann Sawyer. Amanda and James are Avery's great-great-great-great-grandparents on the paternal side.

Both Amanda's headstone and James's family records at Mustang Cemetery are confirmed primary sources. Amanda is confirmed buried at Mustang Cemetery; James's burial location at Mustang Cemetery is the family tradition, though Poole notes his specific grave marker has not been located.

The Wheeler and Thomas families were neighbors and traveled the same migration corridor: Warren County, Georgia → Marengo/Montgomery County, Alabama → Montgomery County (later Grimes County), Texas. They likely knew each other in Alabama before arriving in Texas. Henry H. Wheeler arrived in Texas in November 1840; James Avery Thomas had been there since 1832.

The 1830 Montgomery County, Alabama Census

In the 1830 Alabama census, Henry Wheeler, Green(e) Wheeler, and Jesse Wheeler are listed in adjacent entries in Montgomery County — all living close together. Amanda Wheeler (then ~3 years old) would have been in Henry's household. In the same census, the Henry Wheeler household included a young female child who Poole identifies as almost certainly Amanda.

Henry H. Wheeler (Amanda's Father)

Henry H. Wheeler
b. ~1800, North Carolina  ·  d. after 1854 (exact date unknown)  ·  last found in records 1854, San Patricio County, TX

Father of Amanda Wheeler. Born in North Carolina around 1800 — he stated this himself in the 1850 census. Married Mary Ann Sawyer on 29 August 1822 in Baldwin County, Georgia. After Mary Ann apparently died (possibly ~1835–1840), he married Eliza Holman on 5 January 1840 in Montgomery County, Alabama.

Arrived in Texas in November 1840 — documented in his own land certificate application to the Board of Land Commissioners for Montgomery County, filed 2 May 1842. He and his son John Wheeler filed on the same day.

In the 1850 Grimes County census, Henry H. Wheeler and Mary Ann Wheeler (age 15) are listed in the James Avery Thomas household. Henry is listed as a laborer. The Mary Ann Wheeler listed there is believed by Poole to be a previously unknown daughter of Henry — probably the Mary Ann (Wheeler) Johnson who later married Joseph B.T. Johnson and then James May, and is also buried at Mustang Cemetery.

Henry's Son John Wheeler — A Family Tragedy

John Wheeler (Henry's son, Amanda's brother) married Elizabeth Landrum and lived near the Thomas family in Grimes County. By 1858, John had filed a court action declaring his wife Elizabeth mentally incompetent. She was declared a lunatic and a pauper, and a guardian was appointed for her estate. By 1860, John himself had either died or disappeared — Elizabeth was living in the James Avery Thomas household, listed as insane. Their children (James Avery Wheeler and Sarah Jane Wheeler) were distributed among neighbors.

John Wheeler died intestate in Montgomery County, Texas around December 1859, based on a probate packet filed that month. His son James Avery Wheeler (b. 1852) lived to adulthood; his daughter Sarah Jane Wheeler (b. 5 Sep 1855) survived and married William Franklin Martin in 1870.

Henry Wheeler's Land Dealings

Henry H. Wheeler's land situation was complex. He received a 640-acre headright certificate for arriving in November 1840, but quickly sold off portions of it. By 1847 he had transferred his headright certificate to John B. Harris, who sold it to William Von Rosenberg, who eventually received three separate patents (Kaufman, Llano, and Williamson counties) totaling 640 acres. Henry also sold 122 acres to Samuel Grimmett in July 1843. He appears to have left Grimes County for good sometime after 1851, with a possible survey done in his name in San Patricio County in 1854 — his last known record.

Amanda Wheeler Thomas

Amanda Wheeler
b. 15 May 1827, Georgia  ·  d. 5 Sep 1896, Mustang Prairie, Grimes County, Texas

Daughter of Henry H. Wheeler and Mary Ann Sawyer. Born in Georgia while the family was still in Baldwin County. Arrived in Texas with her father November 1840 at age ~13. Married James Avery Thomas on 28 April 1842 in Montgomery County, Texas, when she was not yet 15.

Amanda outlived James by 31 years. After James died in 1865, she remained on Mustang Prairie and raised the family. She died 5 September 1896 and is buried at Mustang Cemetery. Her headstone is the primary source for her birth and death dates.

Her eight children with James Avery Thomas are detailed in the Thomas Family History summary.

Confirmed by Primary Source

Amanda's birth date (15 May 1827) and death date (5 Sep 1896) are confirmed by her headstone at Mustang Cemetery, photographed and cited by Poole. The marriage date (28 April 1842) is confirmed by Montgomery County, Texas marriage records.

Source: Headstone of "Mrs. Amanda Thomas," Mustang Cemetery, northern Grimes County. Montgomery County, Texas Marriage Records, 1838–1894.

Francis Asbury B. (F.A.B.) Wheeler — Amanda's Uncle

Francis Asbury B. Wheeler ("F.A.B." or "Colonel Wheeler")
b. ~1790–1800, North Carolina  ·  d. ~1841–1842, Montgomery County, Texas

Believed by Poole to be the older brother of Henry H. Wheeler, and therefore Amanda Wheeler's uncle. F.A.B. arrived in Texas in September 1837 (confirmed by land certificate). He settled in Montgomery County in the Lake Creek Settlement area, controlling large amounts of surveyed but unpatented land (~2,114 acres in the 1840 tax rolls). He died intestate, probably in 1841–1842; his widow Susannah Wheeler administered his estate.

F.A.B. Wheeler was elected colonel of the Third Militia District of Texas in June 1840 and led about 150 men in a campaign against raiding Indians — the "Colonel Wheeler's Campaign" documented in Stephen L. Moore's Savage Frontier, 1840–1841. After about four weeks, Wheeler led roughly half his regiment home without engaging the enemy.

F.A.B. Wheeler's Land

F.A.B. Wheeler acquired land rights totaling 1,937+ acres in Montgomery County through a combination of land certificate purchases and a direct headright. The landowner map of the Lake Creek Settlement shows his surveys on both sides of John Landrum's survey — immediately adjacent to the John Thomas (James Avery Thomas's father) survey. This confirms the Thomas and Wheeler families were literal neighbors on adjoining land.

Debunked: F.A.B. Wheeler as Early Pioneer of 1823–1825

Local legends and historical markers claimed F.A.B. Wheeler was "the first settler in 1825 of Montgomery County." Poole disproves this conclusively:

  • He is not listed in Austin's Register of Families, which would have included him if he arrived before 1835
  • His name does not appear on the 1838 Montgomery County tax list
  • He received a Class III land certificate (640 acres) — awarded only to settlers who arrived after 1837
  • The 1850 census ages of his children confirm he was in Georgia and Alabama at least through 1833
  • His first appearance in any Texas record is as a witness to a deed in 1839

Poole notes the claim has been "erroneously repeated so many times that it has become a part of history." The historical marker in Harris County, Georgia naming F.A.B. Wheeler as county coroner is genuine — but it postdates his Texas arrival.

F.A.B. Wheeler's Children

F.A.B. and Susannah Wheeler had three known children: Sarah A.L. Wheeler (who married Allen Lowery), Louisa M. Wheeler, and Amanda Matilda Wheeler — named, notably, after her cousin Amanda Wheeler Thomas. Poole notes this shared name as circumstantial evidence of the close relationship between F.A.B. and Henry H. Wheeler (brothers), and between their daughters.

Jesse Wheeler — Probable Father of Henry and F.A.B.

The most speculative part of the document. Poole believes Henry H. Wheeler and F.A.B. Wheeler were sons of a Jesse Wheeler, who first appears in Baldwin County, Georgia records around 1810. Jesse moved the family from North Carolina to Georgia between 1800 and 1806, then from Georgia to Montgomery County, Alabama, before his sons moved to Texas.

Jesse Wheeler (postulated)
b. ~1760–1775, probably North Carolina  ·  d. ~1830–1840, Montgomery County, Alabama

Poole traces a "Jesse Wheler" in the 1800 Chatham County, North Carolina census, listed in the 26–45 age bracket with two sons — consistent with F.A.B. (b. ~1790) and Henry (b. ~1800) being those sons. Jesse is not found in the 1810 North Carolina census, suggesting he moved to Georgia between 1800 and 1810, which aligns with Baldwin County, Georgia records showing him from ~1810.

In the 1820 Baldwin County, Georgia census, Jesse Wheeler is listed with his two known sons Henry and "Asberry" (F.A.B.) nearby. In the 1830 Montgomery County, Alabama census, Jesse is listed again, now in the 70–80 age bracket, adjacent to Greene Wheeler and Henry Wheeler. Jesse probably died in Montgomery County before 1840, freeing his sons to move to Texas.

Poole explicitly states this relationship "has not in any sense of the word been proven" and may never be provable due to courthouse fires. He presents it as his best current assessment of the circumstantial evidence.

Jesse's Possible Siblings and Origins

Poole traces the Chatham County, NC Wheeler group (Jesse, John, and Dred "Wheler" in the 1800 census) and hypothesizes that these were brothers. He further traces a Joseph Wheeler Sr. of Franklin/Wake counties, NC as a likely father of Jesse. Jesse Wheeler is believed to have been born in North Carolina, possibly with roots going back through Virginia.

Poole also identifies additional possible sons of Jesse beyond Henry and F.A.B.:

  • Greene E. Wheeler (b. 8 Dec 1806, GA; d. 1 May 1879, Harrison Co., TX) — listed adjacent to Jesse and Henry in the 1830 Alabama census
  • Elias H. Wheeler (b. ~1813, GA; d. 31 Dec 1869, Bradley Co., AR) — appears in the same Alabama county cluster
Key Uncertainty

The Wheeler chapter is candid that the family's pre-Georgia origins may never be definitively resolved. The 1800 and 1810 Georgia censuses are lost. Baldwin County, Alabama records were largely destroyed during the Civil War. Poole presents his best working hypothesis while being explicit about what is documented versus inferred.

Debunked Legends — Wheeler Line

F.A.B. Wheeler as First Settler of Montgomery County (1823–1825)

Definitively False

See the F.A.B. Wheeler section above. Land records, tax records, Austin's Register, and census birth data for his children all confirm F.A.B. arrived in Texas no earlier than 1837. The "first settler in 1825" claim appears repeatedly in local histories and historical markers, but has no documentary basis.

Henry H. Wheeler Born in Georgia

Incorrect

One published account (and some family trees) states Henry Wheeler was born in Georgia. Henry himself stated in the 1850 census that he was born in North Carolina. Poole accepts his own testimony and the circumstantial evidence of the family's North Carolina origins.

Henry H. Wheeler in the 1830 Marengo County, Alabama Census

Wrong County

Charlotte Sandel Beck's online account of the Wheeler family stated Henry Wheeler was listed in the 1830 census of Marengo County, Alabama. Poole checked: he was not listed in Marengo County — he was listed in Montgomery County, Alabama. An easy confusion between counties, but a meaningful one for tracing the family's movements.

The Thomas Family Arrived in Texas in 1835 (Wheeler Version)

Incorrect

Poole's footnotes in the Wheeler chapter correct an online account that placed the Thomas family's Texas arrival in 1835. As established in the Thomas chapter, they arrived in December 1832 per Austin's Register. This is noted in the Wheeler chapter footnotes as a factual correction to the Beck account.
