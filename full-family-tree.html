The Thomas Line — Virginia to Texas

The Thomas line is one of the oldest traceable lines in your family, rooted in colonial Virginia before the American nation existed. The earliest confirmed ancestor, Joseph Thomas, was born around 1680 in Nansemond County, Virginia — a county on the south shore of Hampton Roads, settled by English colonists in the 1630s. He married Alice Spivey, born around 1687 in Lancashire, England, suggesting she may have been an immigrant or the daughter of immigrants from northern England.

Over the following 150 years, the Thomas family moved steadily south and west — the classic pattern of English colonial families following the agricultural frontier: from Virginia to North Carolina, then Georgia, then Alabama, and finally into Texas. The generation directly above James Avery Thomas is now confirmed: John Thomas (b. ~1784, South Carolina) and wife Phoebe Springer registered with Austin's Colony in 1835 and settled in what became Grimes County. Their son James Avery Thomas (b. 1812, Marengo Co., Alabama) is your direct 4th great-grandfather and Avery's namesake.

Nansemond County, Virginia — Where It Begins

Nansemond County was one of the original Virginia counties, established in 1637 from a larger county. It sits on the south bank of the James River and Hampton Roads, across from Norfolk. English settlement of the area began in the 1620s–1630s. Joseph Thomas being born there around 1680 places him squarely in the third generation of Virginia colonists — his grandparents likely came directly from England. Alice Spivey's Lancashire origins suggest a northwest England connection; Lancashire was a heavily Catholic and then Protestant contested county, and many Lancashire families emigrated to Virginia in the 17th century.

The Alabama → Texas Migration

Jim Thomas (b.~1812, Alabama) represents the generation that made the jump to Texas. Texas statehood came in 1845, and the decade following saw enormous migration from Alabama, Tennessee, and Georgia — the "Alabama fever" that swept the Deep South as cotton planters and their families sought new fertile land. His daughter (or descendant) Marian F. Thomas was born in Bedias, Grimes County, Texas around 1852, firmly establishing the family in the new state.

Grimes County, where Bedias is located, is in east-central Texas — the first region of Texas settled by Americans coming from Louisiana and Alabama in the 1820s–1840s. The Thomas family followed this exact migration path.

The Skversky → Squires Name Change

The Americanization of Skversky to Squires is a classic pattern among Jewish immigrants of the early 20th century. The name Skversky (also spelled Skvierski, Skwierski) derives from Skwierzyna, a town in what is now western Poland — or possibly from the Yiddish pronunciation of a Russian place name. Upon immigration, names were often changed at Ellis Island by immigration officers who couldn't spell or pronounce Slavic/Yiddish names, or by families themselves seeking faster assimilation. "Squires" as a phonetic approximation of "Skversky" (both starting with Sk/Sq) is consistent with this pattern.

Krawczyk — A Jewish Family in a Polish Town

The Krawczyk name is puzzling at first glance: it is a native Polish word meaning "tailor" (krawiec), and it was commonly assigned to Jewish families in the Pale of Settlement during name-assignment drives under Russian and Prussian rule in the late 18th and early 19th centuries. Jewish families in Kock and surrounding towns were often required to take Polish or German surnames, and occupational surnames like Krawczyk (tailor), Goldstein (goldsmith), or Schneider (also tailor) were common assignments. That your ancestor Abram Jojna carried this name in Kock is consistent with this history.

Kock itself was a small but historically significant shtetl in the Lublin region of Russian-controlled Poland. In the 19th century it was home to a substantial Jewish community — by some estimates Jews made up over half the town's population — and it was a center of Hasidic Judaism, associated with the famous Rabbi Menachem Mendel of Kotzk (the "Kotzker Rebbe"), one of the most influential Hasidic masters of the 19th century. Whether your Krawczyk ancestors were Hasidic or followed other Jewish traditions is unknown, but they lived and died in this world.

Abram Jojna Krawczyk (b. ~1843) and his wife Sura Kuperszlag (b. ~1844) raised a large family in Kock spanning nearly four decades — their oldest known child, Fannie (Feiga), was born in 1870, and their youngest known children (Jack, William, Sarah) were born as late as 1903–1908, suggesting either a long marriage or a second generation that stayed in Kock well into the 20th century. At least seven children are now documented. The span of births — 1870 to 1908 — captures the full arc of the Jewish crisis in the Russian Empire: from relative stability through the devastating pogroms of the 1880s and early 1900s that drove most of the family to emigrate.

Notably, some of Bernard's siblings retained the original Slavic form of the name long after emigration. Samuel and Sarah are recorded as Croft (Kravchik) — preserving the Yiddish pronunciation of Krawczyk ("Kravchik") alongside the Americanized form. This dual naming is a telling window into the immigrant experience: families straddling two worlds, using the anglicized name in daily American life while the old name survived in family documents, synagogue records, and memory.

The Krafcheck Family — Two Towns, One Migration

The Krafcheck family roots run through two closely connected towns in Russian-controlled Poland: Radom and Radzyn Podlaski. Hyman Krafcheck (b. 15 Jun 1873) came from Radom — a substantial industrial city in the Lublin region with a Jewish population that would eventually reach nearly 30,000 before the Holocaust. His wife Libby (Leba) Lipschitz came from Radzyn Podlaski, a smaller town about 100 km to the east, also with a significant Jewish community. Their marriage brought together two established Ashkenazi families from this region.

Together Hyman and Libby had at least seven children, all born in Radzyn Podlaski between roughly 1881 and 1913. The fact that children were still being born there as late as 1913 — after the worst of the pogroms — suggests the family delayed emigration longer than many, or that some siblings emigrated while others remained. Mollie (b. 1905), who eventually married Jack Croft and became Avery's 2nd great-grandmother, was the middle child of this large family. Her siblings — Liba Ruchla, Chane Sura Hanna, Moses, Pesa Bessie, and Scheindle Jean — represent a generation caught between the old world and the new. Whether all of them survived the 20th century's catastrophes in Poland is not yet known.

The name Krafcheck itself is an Americanized spelling of a Yiddish/Hebrew name — likely derived from Kravchik or a similar form, the same root as Krawczyk. This is a striking detail: the Krawczyk family (Croft) and the Krafcheck family share a common etymological root meaning "tailor." Two Jewish families from the same region, both with names meaning tailor, whose children — Jack Croft and Mollie Krafcheck — married each other in America. Whether this is coincidence, a common occupational origin, or something more is an open research question.

The Immigration Story — From the Russian Empire to America

Your maternal Jewish ancestors lived in the Pale of Settlement — the vast zone of the Russian Empire where Jews were legally required to reside, stretching across modern-day Poland, Lithuania, Belarus, Ukraine, and Moldova. Life in the Pale was defined by poverty, periodic pogroms (mob violence against Jews), legal restrictions on where Jews could live and work, and quotas limiting their access to education and professions.

The wave of Jewish immigration to America between roughly 1880 and 1924 (when the Immigration Act of 1924 nearly closed the door) brought approximately 2 million Ashkenazi Jews — the largest Jewish migration in history. Your family was part of this wave.

1840s–1870s
Origins in the Pale — Three Families, Three Towns
Three separate Jewish families are living in the Russian-controlled Pale of Settlement, all unknowingly connected by the descendants they will one day share.

In Kock, Poland: Abram Jojna Krawczyk (b. ~1843) and Sura Kuperszlag (b. ~1844) are raising a family in one of the most famous Jewish shtetls in the Lublin region — a town still resonating with the legacy of the Kotzker Rebbe. Their oldest known child, Fannie (Feiga), is born in 1870. More children will follow across the next four decades.

In Radzyn Podlaski: Wulf Lipszyc (b. 1845) and Bessie Lipschitz (b. 1841) are raising their family, including daughter Chaia Rywka (b. 1867) and Libby (b. ~1881). Libby will eventually marry Hyman Krafcheck (b. 1873, Radom) — bringing two Lublin-region families together.

Somewhere in western Russia: Ben J. Skversky (b. Sep 1859) and Rosie Cohen (b. ~1862, Russia) are living in the Pale. They will eventually have at least nine children. All of these families share the same world: Yiddish as their daily language, the rhythm of the Jewish calendar, the constant weight of legal restrictions on where they may live, work, and travel.
1881–1906
The Pogrom Years — The World Breaks Open
The assassination of Tsar Alexander II in 1881 triggers a wave of violent pogroms across the Pale — Jewish communities burned, looted, attacked, with little or no protection from Russian authorities. A second, even more devastating wave strikes in 1903–1906 (Kishinev, Odessa, and hundreds of other towns). Over two million Jews flee the Russian Empire between 1880 and 1924.

The Skversky family's birth records tell the story directly: Simon Skversky is born in the Soviet Union (Russia) in May 1883; but Harry is born in Pennsylvania the same year — Feb 1883 — suggesting the family crossed the Atlantic right at the start of the pogrom era, or that the births straddle the emigration itself. Frank, Meyer, and David Skversky are all born in Pennsylvania in the late 1880s–1890s, confirming the family settled in the Philadelphia area early. Louie and Minie appear to have been born in Russia, then emigrated as young children.

Bernard "Baruch" Croft (b. 1877, Kock) — Avery's 3rd great-grandfather — makes the crossing in this era, along with his wife Fannie (Feiga) Lampart (b. 1870). His younger siblings Samuel (b. 1896), Tobe Bella (b. 1900), Jack (b. 1903), William (b. 1905), and Sarah (b. 1908) are still being born in Kock, suggesting part of the family delayed emigration. The Krafcheck family in Radzyn Podlaski also stays longer — Mollie is born there in 1905, and her younger siblings Moses (1908), Pesa Bessie (1909), and Scheindle Jean (1913) are born there as well. These families were slower to leave, perhaps unable to afford the passage, perhaps hoping conditions would improve.
1903–1914
Jack and Mollie — Born in the Old World
Jack Croft (born Krawczyk, b. 5 Apr 1903, Kock) and Mollie Krafcheck (b. 15 May 1905, Radzyn Podlaski) are both born in Poland — firmly in the old country, not America. This is a critical detail: unlike some immigrant-generation children born shortly after arrival, Jack and Mollie spent their earliest years in the shtetl world before emigrating. Jack was born while Kock still bore the full weight of the Russian Empire; Mollie's birth year falls between the two great pogrom waves.

At some point — likely between 1905 and 1920, though the exact year is unknown — both families made the crossing. The port of entry for many Polish Jewish families was Philadelphia, not just Ellis Island, via Hamburg–America or Cunard lines through Liverpool. Philadelphia had a large, established Jewish immigrant community, and it is where both the Croft and Skversky families planted roots. Jack and Mollie likely met there, in the tight-knit immigrant Jewish community of Philadelphia.
1920s–1930s
Americanization — Names, Marriage, and the Move to Texas
Jack Krawczyk/Croft marries Mollie Krafcheck. The name Krawczyk has been Americanized to "Croft" — and sometimes "Craft" (Jack appears as "Jack Craft" in some records), showing the name was still settling. The Skversky family has already Americanized to "Squires" by this generation. Both families are part of the Philadelphia Jewish community, navigating American life in the era of the great immigrant assimilation push.

The most striking fact about this generation: Jack and Mollie's children are born not in Philadelphia but in Houston, Texas. Evelyn Croft is born in Houston in 1929; Barbara in 1936; Kenneth around 1938. Something drew the family south — possibly work in the oil industry, family connections, or community ties that remain to be researched. By the 1930s, the Croft family is Texan.

Meanwhile the 1924 Immigration Act has slammed the door nearly shut on Eastern European Jewish immigration. The relatives who did not leave Poland in time — Mollie's siblings Moses, Pesa Bessie, Scheindle Jean; Jack's siblings William and Sarah; all the cousins and aunts and uncles who stayed behind — are now trapped. What happened to them in the 1930s and 1940s is not yet documented in this research, but Radzyn Podlaski and Kock were both devastated in the Holocaust. This is an open and painful research question.

The Name Changes — Identity in a New World

Two major name changes mark your family's Jewish immigration story — and together they reveal two very different strategies for becoming American. One name was simplified phonetically; the other was replaced with something aspirational. Both erased a piece of the old world in exchange for a foothold in the new one. But the old names survived in documents, and now, through genealogy, they have come back.

Krawczyk → Croft      Kravchik → Croft      Skversky → Squires
Krawczyk → Croft (and Kravchik → Croft)

Krawczyk (pronounced roughly "KRAVCH-ik" in Yiddish-inflected Polish) is a Polish occupational surname meaning "tailor" — from krawiec, one who cuts and sews cloth. It was one of the most common surnames assigned to Jewish families during the forced name-registration drives of the late 18th and early 19th centuries under Russian and Prussian rule. The irony of a Jewish family being named "tailor" is multilayered: tailoring was one of the few occupations Jews were legally permitted to practice in many parts of the Pale, so the surname may have reflected a genuine family trade, not merely a bureaucratic assignment.

The Americanization to Croft is not a semantic translation — there is no English equivalent of the tailor concept in "croft." Instead it is phonetic: "Croft" preserves the hard Kr-/Cr- opening consonant cluster and compresses the rest of the name into a clean, English-sounding monosyllable. "Croft" is an Old English word meaning a small enclosed field or smallholding — a thoroughly respectable, rural English sound, evoking land and permanence rather than immigrant newcomer status.

Critically, the family also used the spelling "Craft" — Jack appears as "Jack Craft" in some American records. This tells us the name was still in flux; it had not yet fully settled into a single spelling. And some siblings retained the Yiddish form: Samuel and Sarah are recorded as Croft (Kravchik) — writing the Americanized name first, but preserving the old pronunciation in parentheses. This dual-naming practice is a small window into a family caught between two identities, uncertain which name would stick.

What is particularly striking in this family's case is that Mollie Krafcheck — who married Jack Croft — also had a name derived from the same root. Krafcheck is an Americanized spelling of Kravchik, itself a Yiddish diminutive of kravets (tailor). Two families, both named "tailor" in different linguistic forms, met and married in America. Whether this is coincidence, a shared occupational origin in the Lublin tailoring trade, or something more is an open question worth pursuing.

Skversky → Squires

Skversky is almost certainly a place-name surname — derived from Skwierzyna (a town in what is now western Poland, known in Yiddish as Schwerin an der Warthe), or possibly from another town with a similar root. Place-name surnames were extremely common among Ashkenazi Jews in Eastern Europe; families were often named for the town their ancestors came from, and when they emigrated, those place names traveled with them as surnames.

The Americanization to Squires is a more ambitious transformation than Krawczyk → Croft. The phonetic connection is real — both Sk- and Sq- produce the same initial sound cluster — but the choice of "Squires" goes beyond phonetics. In English social tradition, a squire was a man of minor gentry, a landowner, a person of local standing — a step below knight but above common farmer. It was a title of respect and rootedness. For a Jewish immigrant family from Russia arriving with little but their wits and their work ethic, choosing the name "Squires" was an act of American aspiration: we are not refugees, we are landowners, we belong here.

The timeline of this change is revealing. Ben Skversky's children born in Pennsylvania — Harry (1883), Frank (1887), Meyer (1890), David (1893) — all appear in records as Skversky. But by the time Harry's grandson Arthur was born, the family was thoroughly "Squires." The change happened across two generations, not overnight. Simon Skversky, born in Russia in 1883, may never have made the change at all — his birth record still shows the original name, and his American fate is unknown.

The name "Squires" endures today as Avery's mother's maiden name — Heather Denise Squires — which means that every time that name appears, it carries within it the echo of the Skversky family's crossing from Russia to Pennsylvania, and their deliberate choice to plant themselves in America with a new name that said: we are here, and we are staying.

Why Name Changes Happened — The Full Picture

Name changes among Jewish immigrants were not a single event but a process — driven by overlapping pressures that played out differently in each family and each generation:

  • Immigration processing: Ellis Island and Philadelphia port clerks wrote names phonetically as they heard them, often permanently anglicizing them on official documents before families had any say. A Yiddish-speaking immigrant pronouncing "Krawczyk" might have it written down as "Croft" or "Craft" simply because that's what the clerk heard.
  • Anti-Semitism and passing: A visibly Jewish name could cost someone a job, an apartment, or social standing. Discrimination in hiring was open and legal; landlords routinely refused Jewish tenants. Changing a name to something anglicized was a survival strategy as much as an assimilation one.
  • American identity: Especially among the second generation — children born in America who went to American schools, made American friends, spoke English without an accent — there was enormous pressure to assimilate completely. A name was the most visible marker of foreignness. Changing it was part of becoming "American."
  • Legal and commercial simplicity: Government documents, business contracts, deeds, and tax records all required names that American officials could spell and record. A family running a business quickly found that an unpronounceable name was a practical obstacle.
  • Gradual family drift: Often no single dramatic decision was made. One sibling spelled it one way, another sibling spelled it differently, a court record used a third variant, and over a generation the old name simply faded. The Croft/Craft variation in your family is a perfect example — both spellings were used simultaneously, suggesting the family itself hadn't settled on a final form.
  • What was lost — and what survived: The price of these name changes was the severing of a direct link to ancestral origins. A person named "Croft" has no obvious connection to Kock, Poland, or to the Krawczyk tailoring families of the Lublin region. Genealogical research — like this project — is the act of recovering those severed connections, following the paper trail back through variant spellings to the original name and the original place.
The Names That Did Not Change — Krafcheck

Not every name in this family was fully anglicized. Krafcheck — Mollie's maiden name — is itself already a partial anglicization of Kravchik, but it never completed the journey to a fully English name the way Krawczyk became Croft. Mollie Krafcheck married Jack Croft and took his name, so the Krafcheck name did not pass directly to Barbara or Heather. But it survives in the genealogy as one of the most traceable threads — because "Krafcheck" is unusual enough that it appears in records consistently, unlike the more thoroughly anglicized names that can be harder to trace.

Similarly, the given names in this family show the same pattern of dual identity. Bernard's Yiddish name was Baruch — a Hebrew name meaning "blessed" — and he went by "Baruch Harry," using the American "Harry" alongside the Hebrew original. Fannie's birth name was Feiga (a Yiddish bird name, related to the German Vogel). Jack's Yiddish name may have been Yaakov or a variant. These given name pairs — Baruch/Harry, Feiga/Fannie — are fingerprints: they appear in different records depending on the context, and knowing both forms is what makes it possible to follow a single person through American documents.

The Hasidic Connection — Kock and Radzyn Podlaski

Two of your family's origin towns — Kock and Radzyn Podlaski — are specifically famous as centers of Hasidic Judaism. The Kocker Rebbe (Menachem Mendel of Kotzk, 1787–1859) was one of the most radical and celebrated Hasidic masters, known for his fierce demands for honesty and his legendary 20-year self-imposed isolation. The Radzyner Rebbe line was known for reinstating the tekhelet (blue thread) in the tallit. Whether your Krawczyk and Lipschitz ancestors were Hasidic Jews or opposed to Hasidism (the Mitnagdim movement) is unknown — but they lived in communities saturated with this religious ferment. The world they left behind was one of intense spiritual life, Yiddish literature, and communal solidarity that was largely destroyed in the Holocaust.
