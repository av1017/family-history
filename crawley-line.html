Origins — Simsboro, Louisiana

The Crawley and Robison families both had roots in Simsboro, Lincoln Parish, Louisiana — a small town in the north-central part of the state. Lottie Robison was born there in 1902, the daughter of James A. Robison and Norma Hodge Robison. The Robison family kept the Simsboro property as an ancestral gathering place well into the mid-20th century, and the Crawley children returned there for family reunions through the 1930s, 1940s, and even into the 1960s.

The Great Depression forced John and Lottie out of Louisiana. Jobless, they lived with relatives in Marshall, Texas — a stop on the road that thousands of Depression-era families made, following work wherever it appeared. When the East Texas oil boom erupted around Longview and Gladewater in 1931–32 — at the time the largest oil field in the world — John found work and the family planted roots in the Gladewater area. They eventually settled in Longview, where John worked for Magnolia Petroleum Company.

The East Texas Oil Boom — Why the Crawleys Came to Texas

On October 3, 1930, wildcatter C.M. "Dad" Joiner struck oil near Kilgore, Texas — discovering the East Texas Oil Field, which stretched under Longview, Gladewater, Kilgore, and Tyler. By 1932 it was producing a third of all U.S. oil output. The oil derricks were so dense they were visible in the fields behind the Crawley children's house. Thousands of families followed the work, transforming small East Texas towns into boomtowns practically overnight. John Crawley was one of those workers — and Gladewater became home.

John Earl Crawley & Lottie Robison

John Earl Crawley and Lottie Robison married in 1925. John (b. 8 Feb 1908, Texas · d. Jul 1953) worked for Magnolia Petroleum Company in Longview and registered for the WWII draft in 1942. He died in July 1953 at age 45, leaving Lottie to raise their children and eventually become a social worker. Lottie (b. 11 May 1902, Simsboro, Louisiana · d. Jul 1991, Norman, Oklahoma) outlived John by nearly 40 years and was known to Avery's generation as "Grandma Crawley." She was buried at Elmira Chapel Cemetery, Longview, Texas.

James B. Crawley — US Air Corps / Air Force

James B. Crawley served in the United States Air Corps (later Air Force). Exact dates of service and his family are not yet documented. Charlotte Thomas recalled a family visit to see James while he was stationed — they stayed in a rustic cabin, ate chicken-fried steak canned by an "Aunt Imogene," and camped out one night near what appeared to be a mountain or canyon setting. This visit is believed to be the occasion of the 1964 family group photograph.

Charlotte Lucille Crawley — m. J.G. Thomas Jr.

Charlotte (b. 1931, Louisiana) married J.G. Thomas Jr. on 10 April 1954 at Hill Cumberland Church. J.G. Thomas served as an usher at her brother James's 1956 wedding to Mary Williamson — an early sign of the families' close connection. Charlotte and J.G. had two children: Michael Scott Thomas (Avery's father) and Mary Beth Thomas (m. Reidy).

Mary Anne Crawley — Growing Up in the Oil Fields

Mary Anne was born in 1929 in Haynesville, Claiborne Parish, Louisiana, the eldest of John and Lottie's three children. The family's roots were in Simsboro, where Lottie grew up — but the Depression forced the family out. Jobless, they lived with relatives in Marshall, Texas while Mary Anne was still a toddler. When the East Texas oil boom erupted around Longview and Gladewater in 1931–32, John found work and the family planted roots in the Gladewater area. Mary Anne spent her childhood and teenage years there, with oil derricks visible in the fields behind the house.

For school, the family moved to Spring Hill (between Gladewater and Longview) so Mary Anne could attend Spring Hill School. Her school portraits survive from 1942–43 and 1943–44. Her favorite music was Bob Wills & the Texas Playboys — fitting for a girl growing up in the Texas swing era. A photograph from 1943 shows her laughing outdoors with a girlfriend, and a mid-1940s photo shows her paddling a canoe with a flower in her hair.

Some branches of the extended Crawley family were migrant workers, following seasonal labor across the region — a pattern documented in Charles Convis Jr.'s memorial video with photographs of truck caravans and makeshift trailer homes.

The family continued to return to Simsboro as a home base for large Crawley gatherings. A 1937 photograph shows a group of roughly 13 extended family members assembled at the Simsboro house — one man is in a wheelchair, identity unknown. A 1942 photograph at Simsboro shows Lottie, James, Mary Anne, Charlotte, and a young man in Army uniform — likely a cousin or family friend departing for WWII service. By 1963, Mary Anne and Chuck were bringing their own children to Simsboro; Chuck filmed the visit on 8mm home movie film.

Sources: Charles Convis Jr. memorial video "2MomYouthSLIDES6" (2022). Photos credited to Lottie Crawley and Norma Robison; film by Charles Convis Sr.

Family Photographs

Crawley Family Group Photo — Mountain Setting, ~1964

A large family group photograph, approximately 13 people, likely taken during a family visit to James B. Crawley while he was serving in the Air Corps / Air Force. Charlotte Thomas commented on this photo in the Crawley Family Connection Facebook group: "all I remember about James in the Air Corp was a visit we made to see him. I remember we stayed in a rustic cabin and had a meal of chicken fried steak and gravy that Aunt Imogene had canned. Also, we camped out one night and your mother and I crawled through the car window when we thought there was an animal around our cots."
