The Thomas Arrival in Texas — 1835

The following narrative is drawn directly from History of Grimes County (1982), pages 554–555, cited as Exhibit A in the SRT application. It is the most detailed primary-source account of your Thomas ancestors' arrival in Texas.

Signer of the Texas Declaration of Independence
David Thomas was a delegate to the Convention at Washington-on-the-Brazos and a signer of the Texas Declaration of Independence, signed March 2, 1836. Family tradition holds that he was a brother of your ancestor James Avery Thomas. Multiple Thomas family historians have claimed this connection, but it has not been definitively proven — see the note below for details.
1812
James Avery Thomas born, Marengo County, Alabama
James Avery Thomas is born in Marengo County, Alabama, one of several children of a Thomas family in the Deep South. His siblings will accompany him to Texas more than two decades later.
1835
The Thomas Family Arrives in Texas
James Avery Thomas left Marengo County, Alabama with his brothers and sisters, leaving their parents (Phebe and John) behind in Alabama. They arrived in Texas in 1835, settling west of Conroe in Stephen F. Austin's colony — among the earliest wave of American colonists in Texas. Friends from Alabama had arrived even earlier. Some of the brothers served in Sam Houston's Army during the Texas Revolution.
1836
David Thomas Signs the Texas Declaration of Independence
David Thomas was a delegate to the Convention at Washington-on-the-Brazos, held March 1–17, 1836. On March 2, 1836, the delegates signed the Texas Declaration of Independence. Family tradition claims he was James Avery's brother. However, the evidence is ambiguous: the historical David Thomas is recorded as born in Tennessee (~1800) and arrived in Texas at age ~35 in 1835 — while James Avery's brother David (son of John Thomas and Phoebe Springer) was born in 1816 and was only 14 at Texas arrival. These are likely two different people. At least four Thomas family historians have asserted the connection, but it remains unproven. James Avery, meanwhile, made shoes for the soldiers of the Texas Revolution.
~1836–1842
Land Grant at Mustang Prairie
James Avery Thomas received a grant of land in present Grimes County at Mustang Prairie, in the eastern part of the county. The Thomas family settled there and began farming.
4/28/1842
Marries Amanda K. Wheeler
James Avery Thomas married Amanda K. Wheeler on April 28, 1842 in Grimes County. Together they would have eight children.
1846–1862
Eight Children Born
James Avery and Amanda had eight children, all born in Grimes County, Texas: John Henry (1846), Kathyron (1850), Francis Marion (1852), David (1855), Mack (1857), Arch (1860), Sam (1862), and Phoebie (no date). All settled around this part of the county, buying land and farming.
1865
James Avery Thomas dies, Grimes County
James Avery Thomas died in 1865 in Grimes County, Texas, at age 53. Amanda K. Wheeler survived him; she died September 5, 1896.
"I am a descendant of James Avery Thomas (1812–1865). He left Marengo County, Alabama with his brothers and sisters to come to Texas, leaving their mother and father, Phebe and John, in Alabama. They arrived in 1835, west of Conroe, in Stephen F. Austin's colony. Friends from Alabama had arrived earlier. Some of the boys served in Sam Houston's Army. David was a delegate to the convention at Washington-on-the-Brazos, and a signer of the Declaration of Independence. James Avery made shoes for the soldiers. He was given a grant of land in present Grimes County at Mustang Prairie in eastern part of the county. He married Amanda Wheeler in 1842." — Obie R. Thomas, in History of Grimes County (1982), p. 555 · Exhibit A, Thomas Lineal Descent.pdf

David Thomas (signer) — is he James Avery's brother? Family tradition says yes, and at least four Thomas family historians have claimed it. However, the historical David Thomas (signer of the Declaration) is recorded as born in Tennessee around 1800 and arriving in Texas in 1835 at approximately age 35 — whereas John Thomas's son David was born in 1816 and was only 14 at Texas arrival. These birth years are irreconcilable — they are most likely two different people who happened to share a name and move to the same region. The question remains an open research issue; the David Thomas Papers at UT Austin (Dolph Briscoe Center) would be the best resource to resolve it definitively.

Anthony Drew Kennard — Biography

The following is drawn from the biographical sketch in the Grimes County Historical Newsletter (February 2016), compiled from family records and published by Vanessa Kierce Burzynski.

Fact Detail
Born 9 July 1788, Maryland
Married Sarah Moore
Settled "Ross' Prairie" (also called "Kennard's Prairie"), Grimes County, Texas
Land grant Received a league of land from the Mexican Government — documented in a Spanish-language land grant (original document in the PDF)
Military service Joined General Burleson's military expedition to meet and arrange a treaty with the Tehuacana Indians
Texas Revolution Fought through the Texas Revolution until Texas independence was won
Died 1848 — Sarah Moore became a widow; a single marker identifies their graves
Children John H. Kennard (m. Hattie Brown), Ligie (m. Jack Hirst), Mary Lou, Michael Moore Kennard
1788
Born in Maryland
Anthony Drew Kennard born July 9, 1788 in Maryland. He would be among the first Anglo-American settlers in what would become Grimes County, Texas.
~1820s–1830s
Texas Colonization — Mexican Land Grant
Kennard received a league of land from the Mexican Government as part of the colonization of Texas under empresario Stephen F. Austin. A league was approximately 4,428 acres — an enormous grant. The original Spanish-language land grant document is preserved in the PDF collection. He settled his land in what became known as "Ross' Prairie" or "Kennard's Prairie," the area around present-day Anderson, Texas.
~1834–1835
Burleson Expedition — Tehuacana Indians
Kennard joined General Edward Burleson's military expedition in an attempt to council and arrange a treaty with the Tehuacana Indians. The Tehuacanas were a Wichita-related group whose territory overlapped with the new Anglo settlements. Burleson's expedition was part of the broader effort to secure the frontier before the Texas Revolution.
1835–1836
Texas Revolution
When conflict between Texas and Mexico broke out, Kennard fought through the war until Texas won its independence. The document notes he "took little interest in local [politics]" before the war but "fought well through the war."
1848
Death — Sarah Moore widowed
Anthony Drew Kennard died in 1848, having lived to see the Republic of Texas become a U.S. state (1846). His wife Sarah Moore survived him. A single grave marker identifies both their graves. Sarah Moore died in 1877 at approximately 87 years of age.

The Kennard Family Home

The biographical sketch in the newsletter includes a description of the Kennard family home, built in 1892 — well after Anthony Drew Kennard's death, likely by his son John H. "Papa" Kennard or the next generation.

Description of the Kennard Home

A wooden frame, two-story house with a three-bay front, gable roof, shed-roofed porch at front, and lean-to rooms at rear with one gable end. The chimney was stone. Built in 1892, located 2 miles north of Anderson, Texas (Grimes County). The document includes photographs of the home, which are preserved as image pages in the PDF (pages 5–13) but could not be fully extracted by OCR.

The Kennard Family Letters — 1860s

Two private family letters were preserved in the document collection. Both date from the early 1860s, immediately before and during the Civil War. They give a rare first-person window into daily life, family economics, and political sentiment in Grimes County, Texas during this era.

Historical Context
These letters mention enslaved people by name and document the division of enslaved individuals as property among family members — practices that were central to the plantation economy of the antebellum South. These passages are preserved here as primary historical record. The letters also contain explicit pro-secession sentiment, reflecting the dominant political view in East Texas at the time.

Letter 1 — ~1860 · Sibling to A.D. Kennard Jr.

Written by a Kennard sibling (likely Michael Moore Kennard, based on the signature "M. M. Kennard") to A.D. Kennard Jr., probably around 1860. The letter covers family business, the distribution of enslaved people following their mother's move, local hardships, and a strong declaration of secession sentiment.

"Mother has sold her place to Mr. [Fanthorp] for two hundred and forty dollars, and has divided out a part of her negroes among the children. Rose and her family were appraised at $3,000 and went to Mr. William Fanthorp; Henrietta and Fox were appraised at $2,000 and came to me. Tor was appraised to John at $1,700. Austin was appraised at $1,500 and set apart for [another child]. She intends to sell her horses and cattle as soon as she can get good prices and wishes [you] would like for you both to be here also." — M. M. Kennard, letter to A.D. Kennard Jr., ~1860 · Anthony Drew Kennard Letters etc..pdf, p. 2
"There is considerable political excitement here but I am happy to say that not with tending hard times that we are nearly all about the right way of thinking politically — when I say right I mean that we are in favor of secession and nothing shorter will satisfy us. Here's for the old 'Lone-Star Republic' once more and forever — have lost all confidence in the union and hope and trust it will be dissolved soon, very soon — sooner the better — and my life with it than submit to a 'Black Republican President.'" — M. M. Kennard, ~1860 · Anthony Drew Kennard Letters etc..pdf, p. 2

Letter 2 — January 4, 1864 · Michael Kennard to A.D. Kennard Jr.

Written during the Civil War by Michael Kennard to his brother A.D. Kennard Jr. Michael's wife and family have been visiting Houston. He describes the hardships of farming and legal work during wartime, the failure of the previous year's crops, and the general state of the community.

"My dear brother, a long time has lapsed since there has been any written correspondence between us and I must confess that at last an equal share of the fault is mine — but it is not worthwhile to fill up half a letter with apologies and excuses, suffice it to say that I know that it has not been for the want of brotherly feeling on my part." — Michael Kennard, letter to A.D. Kennard Jr., 4 January 1864 · Anthony Drew Kennard Letters etc..pdf, p. 3
"My family are now in Houston on a visit to my wife's parents and have been there about two or three weeks — almost three weeks. I have been at home almost alone during that absence. Then you think it is no wonder that I am not very lively tonight... Yours: Well another year has passed away and turned its year of events into the history of the world and whether better or worse one thing is certain we are older — and though there may be much to regret in the undivided history of each yet there is much to be thankful for, for what has been bad might have been worse and with a merciful providence continues to extend to us the great boon of life we may repent, we may improve, and we should be grateful for the opportunity to repent and to improve and use it accordingly — but enough of this moralizing." — Michael Kennard, 4 January 1864 · Anthony Drew Kennard Letters etc..pdf, p. 3
"Very poor crops were made in this part of the country last year. Mine was almost an entire failure, which you know was calculated to discourage a new beginner in the farming business. But I shall not despair yet, I shall try again and harder this year. I have done pretty much all the law business but it has taken really all I could make to support my family owing to a failure in seed crop. And there is no chance now to collect what is owing to me and therefore I am like almost everybody else in these parts — not able to pretend to meet my debts, so everybody or nearly every body is pressed with the reality of hard times." — Michael Kennard, 4 January 1864 · Anthony Drew Kennard Letters etc..pdf, p. 3
Connecting the Letters to Your Family
"Michael Kennard" (the letter writer) is Michael Moore Kennard (b. 27 July 1809, d. 7 July 1895) — son of Anthony Drew Kennard and Sarah Moore, and your 3rd-great-grand-uncle. His brother A.D. Kennard Jr. is also a son of Anthony Drew Kennard. The family member who connects to Avery's direct line is John H. "Papa" Kennard (b. 1852) — Michael's nephew — who married Hattie Brown and whose daughter Stella Kennard married James Gary Thomas.
