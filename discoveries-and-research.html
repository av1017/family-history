Revolutionary War Discovery Confirmed

Bombshell — April 16, 2026
Through Ancestry research, the Thomas line has been traced back to James E. Thomas (b. 1714, Nansemond County, Virginia · d. November 1780, Bertie County, North Carolina) — your 6th great-grandfather. His son James Thomas (2nd) (b. 2/12/1760, Bertie Co., NC) served as a Private in the North Carolina Revolutionary Army, making him your 5th great-grandfather and a Sons of the American Revolution (SAR) qualifying patriot ancestor.

This discovery parallels the SRT (Sons of the Republic of Texas) memberships held by your father Michael Scott Thomas and your first cousin twice removed Richard Gary Thomas — and means you are potentially eligible for SAR membership through this line.

The lineage was surfaced as an Ancestry hint attached to James E. Thomas's profile — a SAR membership application filed by a Kentucky branch of the same Thomas family. That Kentucky family descends from James Thomas (2nd), while your Texas line descends from a different child of James E. Thomas (most likely Joseph Thomas, b. ~1735–1740). Both lines share the same Revolutionary War patriot ancestor.

SAR vs. SRT — Two Patriot Societies
Your family now has documented connections to two patriot lineage societies:

Sons of the Republic of Texas (SRT) — requires lineal descent from a resident of Texas before annexation (Feb 19, 1846). Your father Michael Scott Thomas and Richard Gary Thomas are both members, qualifying through Anthony Drew Kennard and James Avery Thomas respectively.

Sons of the American Revolution (SAR) — requires lineal descent from someone who actively supported American independence during the Revolutionary War (1775–1783). James Thomas (2nd), your 5th great-grandfather, served as a Private in the North Carolina Revolutionary Army. You would be eligible to apply through this line once the connecting generations are fully documented.

The SAR Patriot — James Thomas (2nd)
The actual Revolutionary War patriot in the SAR application is James Thomas (2nd) (b. 2/12/1760, Bertie Co., NC · d. 9/5/1832, Trigg Co., KY) — son of James E. Thomas (1714). He served as a Private in the North Carolina Revolutionary Army. His line went to Kentucky; your Texas line descends from a sibling (likely Joseph Thomas, b. ~1735). The SAR application references DAR Patriot Index #23X and cites sources including: Abstracts of Original Entries, Pulaski County History p.145; History of Thomas & Bridges Family; and North Carolina Revolutionary Army rolls (Reel 438, 432).
Missing Link — The Generation Between James E. Thomas and James Avery Thomas
There is a gap of roughly 100 years between James E. Thomas (d. 1780, Bertie Co., NC) and James Avery Thomas (b. 1812, Marengo Co., AL). At least two intermediate generations are needed. The GEDCOM and Shelly Rhodes' Ancestry tree both show a "Joseph Thomas" and/or "John C. Thomas" filling this gap, but the exact chain is not yet fully confirmed. This is the primary open research question for the Thomas line. Resolving it would allow you to formally document SAR eligibility.

2. What are the intermediate generations between James E. Thomas (d. 1780) and James Avery Thomas (b. 1812)? Open
The SAR application confirms the line back to James E. Thomas (1714). But the connecting generations between him and James Avery Thomas (1812) — spanning roughly South Carolina, Georgia, and Alabama — are not fully documented. The GEDCOM shows "Joseph Thomas" (~1735) and possibly "John C. Thomas" (~1784) filling the gap. These need to be confirmed with census and deed records tracing the Thomas family from Bertie County, NC through SC, GA, and into Alabama.
Best leads: NC → SC → GA deed records · Ancestry hints on Joseph Thomas profile · Shelly Rhodes' tree
3. Is David Thomas (signer of the Texas Declaration of Independence) related to your line? Uncertain
Family tradition holds that David Thomas (signer, 1836) was James Avery Thomas's brother. The History of Grimes County cites this. However, the historical David Thomas is recorded as born ~1800 in Tennessee and arriving in Texas in 1835 at approximately age 35 — whereas John Thomas's son David was born ~1816 and was only 14 at Texas arrival. These are likely two different people. The connection may be a different branch of the same broader Thomas family (not a direct sibling relationship). The David Thomas Papers at UT Austin (Dolph Briscoe Center) are the best resource to resolve this.
Best lead: David Thomas Papers, Dolph Briscoe Center for American History, UT Austin · finding aid: legacy.lib.utexas.edu/taro/utcah/01581
4. Are William Thomas (1765) and Elizabeth Purviance James Avery's grandparents (not parents)? Uncertain
Shelly Rhodes' tree places William Thomas (1765–1833) + Elizabeth Purviance (1765–1849) as James Avery Thomas's parents. But if John C. Thomas (~1784) is James Avery's father, then William and Elizabeth would be his grandparents — one generation further back. William Thomas (b. 1765) being the father of John C. Thomas (b. ~1784) is chronologically plausible (19 years apart). This would make William Thomas + Elizabeth Purviance your 6th great-grandparents from Avery's perspective, not 5th.
Best lead: Georgia deed records · 1800 and 1810 census records for William Thomas in SC/GA/AL
