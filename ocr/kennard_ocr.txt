INTRODUCTION

Why Write A Paper About The Kennards?

During a recent family birthday dinner for my sister with her family, someone asked me
what | had been doing. | responded that ! was writing about the Kennard part of our family. One
of my nieces asked, “Why the Kennards?” Boy, did that surprise me. The reason was so
fundamental to me that ! struggled to explain it.

My niece grew up in Ft. Worth, Texas. She had a close relationship with my mother and
heard many stories about my father, both of whom my niece knew as Thomas’. My niece quite
often visited my mother in Navasota, Texas, but there were no Kennards in Navasota. They all
lived in Anderson, Texas, 10 miles east of Navasota.

On occasion, my sister took her children to Anderson, Texas where they likely visited our
aunt and uncle, but those visits were relatively short, and likely did not include any time with
people with the last name “Kennard.” In light of that background, her question was entirely
reasonable. She never knew or had any kind of meaningful relationship with any Kennards.
What a surprise! There is someone in my family who never really knew any Kennards.

That made me realize that all of my children and grandchildren would have the same
question. In fact, so would all my relatives after my generation. They neither spent any time in
Anderson nor ever knew any Kennards. | never came close to considering that to be the case. It
was a real revelation to me. | better address the question in the paper: “Why the Kennards?”

First, it is important to remember that all of our direct ancestors who do not bear our
last names are equally kin to us as those bearing our last names. Shouldn’t they receive equal

attention from us as we give to our ancestors who bear our last name?


--- PAGE BREAK ---

Secondly, the Kennards were original colonists of Grimes, County, Texas settling there in
1832, a fact worthy of at lest a degree of pride. Anderson, Texas is the County Seat of Grimes
County. My paternal grandmother was a Kennard. She, all of her brothers and sisters, and the
rest of her Kennard family lived in Anderson, Texas all of their lives. My immediate family was
very close to my grandmother and spent a good deal of time with her in Anderson. In my mind,
Anderson, Texas was a type of “Ground Zero” for my immediate family, despite the fact that we
lived 10 miles away in the town of Navasota. My father was one of the first in the entire
Kennard family to move away from Anderson. That was a “big deal” to the Kennards when he
did so. They had always lived in Anderson. My grandmother actively opposed the move and
sought assistance from other Kennard family members to prevent it. Of course, she was not
successful.

Accordingly, | grew up knowing a lot of Kennard kinfolks. My sister lived in Anderson
until she was 9 years old, so she knew a Jot of Kennards and identifies with them too. After
moving to Navasota, she and the rest of my immediate family spent a lot of time in Anderson. |
personally spent a lot of time in Anderson while growing up, often spending weekends and
summers with my paternal grandmother who was a Kennard. Accordingly, both my sister and |
knew many Kennards and spent time with them. From the Kennards, we experienced genuine
family love, Naturally, they were who we thought of first when family came to mind.

In fact, we identified with the Kennards a lot more than we identified with the Thomas’
of Grimes County, even though the Thomas’ had also been in Grimes County a very long time.
One of the main reasons was that no Thomas family members lived in Anderson. They mainly
lived in the northeast part of Grimes County around the community of Shiro (pronounced with
a long “i”), Texas. Although we visited with a great aunt and uncle in Shiro occasionally, we
simply did not form a close relationship with them or any other Thomas’ other than our aunts
and uncles, one of whom lived in Anderson. And his name was “Kennard” Thomas.

Further, our paternal grandfather, Gary Thomas, died when I was 5 years old and had
dementia for a long time before that. Accordingly, | never had the opportunity to hear his

stories about his family and childhood as | did with my Kennard grandmother.


--- PAGE BREAK ---

At the risk of appearing pompous and aloof, we had more in common with the Kennards
than the Thomas’. Whereas the Kennards were rather culturally sophisticated and well
educated for their day, the Thomas’ were less so. Nevertheless, the Grimes County Thomas’
were known to be honest and hard working. | am no less proud of them than any other
ancestors of mine. | just did not identify with them nearly as much as the Kennards.

It should be noted that my grandfather, Gary Thomas, had a rather amazing and
inspiring story of how he came from a very poor farming background to become a college
educated, highly regarded, and well respected banker and cattleman. That is beyond the
purview of this paper, however.

Lastly, this paper is about the Kennards because that family enjoyed a high standing and
good reputation in our home county. Everyone in my immediate family benefitted from that in
many ways. | think that is also true for other Grimes County Kennards. It is logical, therefore,
that the Kennard family is who we mainly identified with. | understood that my last name was
Thomas, but my life experiences and my heart was with the Kennards. These are the reasons
why this paper is about the Kennards.

The Kennards Before Coming to America

The consensus of opinion is that the names “Kennaird,” “Kenward,” and “Kennard” have
their origin in Northern France, which was a part of ancient Gaul.? Northern France was also
inhabited by people of the Celtic race who had invaded the area in the 10" century. Armies of
this mixed race invaded England in 1066 under William The Conqueror.’ The family name,
therefore, came to England during the Norman Conquest as “Kenewardus” or “Keneward,” as

mentioned in the “Doomsday Book” in 1086.° Some of them found their way to Scotland.


--- PAGE BREAK ---

There are a number of “Kenewsards,” “Kennards”, “Kenewarduses” and “Kinnairds” in
English and Scottish history, but apparently, no direct connection was located providing links
between any of them to members of our Kennard family.° According to AKP, family lore is that
our family’s ancestor living near Aberdeen, Scotland supported the “Pretender” in an attempt
to free Scotland from England. When this attempt was defeated, our ancestor lost all of his land

and property and he fled first to Northern Scotland, and later to the Ulster part of Ireland.’

THE VARIOUS SPELLINGS OF “KENNARD”

Initially, it must be recognized that compared to today’s standards, spelling in general
was rather imprecise as late as the latter part of the 1800s and early 1900s. illiteracy or poor
education (by 2018 standards) resulted in a lack of uniformity in spelling. While there were
earlier English dictionaries that were not considered as being thorough, the first comprehensive
Dictionary of the English Language was not published until 1752.° Often, phonetic spelling of
words was a big cause of imprecise spelling. Phonetic spelling was made worse by various
dialects. The spelling of surnames was perhaps even more imprecise than spelling in general.
Surnames could be informally changed from one generation to the next, and sometimes from
one locale to another. This was particularly the case for immigrants whose names were
recorded upon entry to America by clerks.

AKP states that our original Scottish ancestor spelled his last name “Kinnaird.” According
to family lore, upon coming to America, he and his family simplified it by changing the spelling
to “Kennard.” However, an old Scotch schoolmaster scolded them for not using the original
spelling, “Kinnaird.” As a result, one of the brothers changed it back to the original spelling.
Another brother compromised by changing it to “Kinnard.” The last brother did not change and
retained the new “Kennard” spelling. The respective descendants of each brother kept those

spellings of their surnames.”

Kennards In America

1. Who Were Our First Ancestors in America?

Both AKP and EBR agree that the earliest proven ancestors of the Grimes County
Kennards is Richard Kennard and his wife, Mary Pullen Howell, who married in 1693 and lived
in Kent County, Maryland. That represents hard work by the two researchers and traces the
family lineage back a long way. But there are still unanswered questions: from what locale or
country did Richard Kennard come; who were his Parents; how did he or his ancestors get to
America and; from what locale or country did his parents come? From the two primary sources
| used for this paper, the answers are at best unclear and at worst unknown.

AKP does not address these questions in her book. She mentions no ancestor in America
before Richard Kennard. The likely reason is the lack of sound proof of a previous ancestor.

EBR, on the other hand, documents substantial research in search of answers to these
questions. She presents a theory unsupported by direct documentary evidence, but is based
upon circumstantial evidence and logical deductions from known facts. Still this theory is
somewhat vulnerable to being disproved upon discovery of new facts. Simply stated, EBR’s
theory may be wrong. From the resources | have conveniently available to me, { cannot tell one
way or the other.

EBR performed research in New England searching for a direct ancestor to Richard
Kennard of Kent County, Maryland. She found an Edward Kennard who came to Portsmouth,
New Hampshire from Kent County, England, and married Elizabeth Martyn there in 1680 or

D. Michael and Johanna Kennard

Michael Kennard, the son of Nathaniel Kennard II, was born near Havre de Grace,
Harford County, Maryland in 1744." Harford County, Maryland is located on the other side of
Chesapeake Bay from Kent County, Maryland where Nathaniel Kennard fl grew up. It is
presumed that he and his family made the move across Chesapeake Bay. One wonders why
make the move? Was it because family land owned by family members of prior generations
had been diluted because of numerous inheritances? We will likely never know the answer.

In 1765, Michael Kennard married Johanna (“Hanna”) Drew, born on September 25,
1747.” They left Maryland about 1790 for North Carolina when he was 46 years old.”* They
were the first in their family to do so.“ The 1790 Census of Wilmington District, Brunswick
County, North Carolina lists as residents Michael Kennard, four sons over 16 years of age,
three other sons, three females, and five slaves as living there.” Apparently, a brother of
Michael Kennard, Nathaniel “Kinard” (likely Nathaniel Ill), also moved to the same area of
North Carolina and is also listed as a resident in the same 1790 Census.”

After moving to North Carolina, they continued to move southwest. By 1792 or ‘93,
they reached the center of the territory that was soon to become Tennessee’which officially
became a state of the United States on June 1, 1796.” They lived near Murfreesboro (near
Nashville). That area soon became a part of Rutherford County, Tennessee.” The Rutherford
County records are full of Kennard family transactions.©°

Why did they decide to move away from Maryland where their family had lived for at

1. The Move To Texas.

In the late 1790s, Moses Austin emigrated from Connecticut to Missouri when it was still
a province of the Spanish government.© He was a young businessman who was likely always
looking for business opportunities. After experiencing financial reversals caused by banks
collapsing, including the Bank of St. Louis, and a crash in land values around 1818, Moses Austin
dreamed of bringing colonists into Spanish Territory, aiso called “New Spain,” in what is now
Texas.” He contacted Spanish officials in what presently is San Antonio, Texas (then known as
Bexar, pronounced as “Bear.”) and, after experiencing rejection initially, he and a friend, Baron
de Bastrop, persuaded the Spanish authorities to grant him permission to bring “Norte
Americano” (i.e. North American) families to Spanish Texas.’° Two of the successful arguments
used were that (1) for several centuries the Spanish and Mexicans had not come in sufficient

numbers to the area to settle it, and (2) the Indian danger in Texas was the main reason that

15


--- PAGE BREAK ---

discouraged such colonization.”* Americans had a history of successfully battling native Indians
and would provide somewhat of a buffer for future Spanish colonization.

Moses Austin became ill and died before seeing his idea of colonization come into
fruition.” His son, Stephen F. Austin, had been working with his father on this matter and was
recognized by the authorities as the heir of Moses. Thus, the Spanish authorities granted
Stephen F. Austin permission to colonize what would later become Texas. He became the first
and by far the most important organizer or “Empresario” of North American colonists into
Texas.”* He turned out to be quite skillful, well suited, and most successful in this endeavor.
Stephen F. Austin is known as the “Father of Texas.” He was beloved by the vast majority of the
Anglos in Texas, and was their de facto leader. The city of Austin, Texas, the Capital of Texas, is
named for him. if it wasn’t for Stephen F. Austin, Texas may have never been Anglicized and
become a part of the United States.

On August 24, 1821, Mexico won its independence from Spain.” However, this did not
affect Stephen F. Austin’s plans to colonize Texas. In 1822, the first North American colonists
entered into the territory that was then known as Tejas (pronounced Tayhas with a long “a”).”°
This first colony of Austin’s is known as “The Old Three Hundred,” a name well known to all
students who took a Texas history course.

Although Stephen F. Austin was the initial and, by far the most significant and influential
Empresario, there were others. One of the other Empresarios was Sterling Clack Robertson.
Empresarios had enormous power over their colonies. For example, they could appoint various
officials of the colony.”

While in Alabama, Anthony Drew and Sarah Kennard decided to move yet again. This
time to Tejas; i.e., Texas. No doubt the prospect of obtaining large amounts of free land in Tejas
lured them into making yet another move. The decision, however, was a great gamble for
them. They were moving to another country that was completely unsettled and dangerous.
What a monumental decision that was for multiple generations of their descendants.

At that time, Tejas was a part of the State of Coahuila, in the country of Mexico. In

2. The indian Problem For Settlers In Tejas (Texas)

Native Indians have been in Texas, for thousands of years. When the Spanish
government entered Tejas, it created a vast trading network that attracted more Indians to
move to what is now Texas in the early 1700s.°” One of those many tribes was the Tawakonis.®
The opportunity for horse and gun trade motivated more Indians to migrate to Tejas in the late
1790s.” This brought the Apache and Comanche, two of the most savage and fierce Indian
tribes. indian attacks and raids upon what few Spanish colonists came to Tejas were primary
reasons Spain was unsuccessful in colonizing Texas.*™ Please recall that one of the arguments
to the Spanish, and later Mexican, authorities to allow colonists from America into Tejas was to
solve the Indian problems and thereby remove the Indians as an impediment to future
colonization of Spaniards, and later Mexicans, in Texas.

The need to colonize Tejas did not change once Mexico gained its independence from
Spain in 1821.” The colonists were needed to serve as a buffer for the Mexicans against
marauding Indians.” The Kennards soon became involved in that effort.

to be good with knives and guns. “Survival demanded it.”2™ The Indians were a very real threat
to the Anglo-Americans living in Tejas.

3. The Texas War Of Independence From Mexico

The reasons behind the war for Texas’ independence are complex. They include, but are
not limited to: (1) the clash of cultures (Anglo vs. Spanish-Latin American); (2) the change in the
Mexican form of government from one of Federalism where the states had the main power in
government to Centralism where the central government has the main power; (3) the free spirit
of the Anglos; (4) the dominance in Texas of Protestantism over Catholicism; and (5) certain
new Immigrant-Anglos merely seeking adventure.""°

A primary symptom of the general clash of cultures was an investigation by the Mexican
government into Texas led by the elite in Mexico and the subsequent passing of the April 6

law that prohibited further immigration into Tejas from America, which was discussed above.”

20


--- PAGE BREAK ---

One of the primary motivations for that law was ethnic in nature. The elite in Mexico did not
want a culture dominating Tejas that was Western European instead of Latin American. This
was a very consequential event with the existing Tejanos who came from America. It caused
significant animosity among the Anglo colonists against Mexico sparking lasting anti-Mexican
sentiment. The colonists “saw it as a gratuitous insult to their kind.”*"* indeed, the law is said to
have been the stimulus for the Texas War of independence from Mexico.”

When the Americans initially came to Tejas in the early 1820s, all of Mexico was under a
constitution adopted in 1824 that provided for strong state governments; i.e., a “States Rights”
structure where the central government was not dominate.” That was abruptly reversed in
1834 by the new President Santa Anna. He had the Mexican government void the Constitution
of 1824 and adopt a constitution that stripped the power from the states and placed it with the
central government ruled by Santa Anna.” That was unpopular among a significant number of
Mexicans in Mexico. However, it was extremely unpopular with the Anglos and most of the
Hispanics living in Tejas.” This was not a mere theoretical difference of opinion. The new
constitution was close to being despised most Texicans. In fact, Tejas slowly became the refuge
for Mexicans with anti-government sentiments providing both Anglos and Mexicans in Texas
with a united cause.” This explains one of the early Texas Flags in the Texas Revolution that
with a white background simply stated, “1824,” a reference to the States Rights Mexican
Constitution of 1824 that had been repealed.

The Texas colonists were independent by nature. They did not want any help or
involvement from any government. Until Santa Anna’s new constitution of 1834, Texans
experienced self-government, which actually meant no government at all. The Mexican
government simply was not a part of their lives. That changed with the central Mexican
government directly “intervening” in the affairs of Tejas. In 1835, the central government sent
Customs officers to Anahuac and Velasco, Texas. This resulted in violence. The Mexican central
government responded by sending military to quell future uproars.”“4 The Texans were hostile
to a centralized authoritarian form of government.’™ This gave birth to an informal War Party

in Texas that advocated independence from Mexico.

21



--- PAGE BREAK ---

Initially, the War Party organizers and supporters were recent newcomers eager to stir
up trouble just for the sake of adventure. Basically, they were “hotheads,” or “revolutionaries”
such as William Travis, James Bowie, “Three Legged Willie,” and even Sam Houston. They
were not significant landowners who were established and provided structure to the
community. Instead, they were simply adventure seekers and troublemakers.

There were also those who opposed war and a revolution against Mexico. They were a
very loosely organized group that amounted to a Peace Party and were loosely led by Stephen
F. Austin. At this point in time, he strongly opposed any type of insurrection. Length of
residence in Tejas was a major determiner of who opposed violence against Mexico.” This
makes sense because the longer the residence, the more likely substantial assets were acquired
that would be totally at risk in an armed revolution. If everything is going pretty well, why rock
the boat?

The War Party, however, gained steam as the heavy handedness of the central Mexican
government continued to progress into the internal affairs of Texas, including military
excursions into Texas to enforce its will on Texans. This was offensive to the free spirit -
independent nature of the Anglos in Texas.

After a number of skirmishes with Mexican soldiers, a Convention to discuss
independence from Mexico was held at Washington On The Brazos and attended by delegates
of all populated areas of Texas. The Declaration was completed and signed by all delegates on
March 2, 1836.’”° This was during the siege of the Texans at the Alamo. Before signing the
Declaration, a delegate Henry Smith stated to the other delegates:

Fellow-Citizens of Texas: The enemy are upon us. A strong force
surrounds the wails of the Alamo, and threaten that garrison with
the sword...Now is the day, and now is the hour, when Texas expects
every man to do his duty. Let us show ourselves worthy to be free
and we shall be free.?”?

At that point in time, Henry Smith’s statement reflected the general sentiment among

Texans.

a. What Was the Position of the Anthony Drew Kennard Family?

We have no indication that Anthony Drew and his sons were “hotheads” looking to
make trouble for the sake of adventure. They appeared to live peaceably in Tennessee and
Alabama. They were part of the Indian campaign against the Tawakonis, but that does not
equate to them being adventurers. That could be considered as simply trying to make their new
home safe for their family members. By the time of the Texas Revolution they had been in
Texas for only 4 years, but they were owners of significant amounts of land that was producing
a good living for the Kennards. They were probably considered to be among the “established”
in the community making them less likely to want to “rock the boat” with a less certain future
caused by a revolution.

In the end, we do not know where they stood on how Texans should react to the
Mexican centralist government actions in Texas. Even if one assumes the Kennards had no
opinions or even opposed war, like practically all Texans, they soon supported Texas
Independence. This was even the case for the leader of the Peace Party, Stephen F. Austin. He
traveled to Mexico for a diplomatic reason. Tensions had become high in Texas. While in
Mexico seeking peace, he was imprisoned for over a year without a trial, and held
incommunicado without visitors, books to read, or writing materials."2° During this harsh and
undeserved imprisonment, Stephen F. Austin’s views on war changed 180 degrees. Now the
popular de facto leader of the Texans became very much in favor of revolution and Texas’
independence from Mexico.*** Further, the general “War Party” movement had gained
momentum and the sentiment of the populace in Texas was clearly moving toward a war of
independence from Mexico.

We do not know the precise attitude of the Grimes County Kennards toward war as it

surrendered, but were summarily executed, and their bodies piled up and burned.” Only one

4. Texas Becomes a State of the United States of America

Texas was an independent nation for over 9 years, from 1836 until the end of 1845.
Mexico never fully accepted Texas’ independence. In fact, Mexico made a few military
excursions into the Republic of Texas during those 9 years. William Everett Kennard who
participated in the San Jacinto campaign was involved in two of them. This will be discussed
later in this paper.

The matter of annexing Texas was debated in the U. S. Congress for quite a while. Even
that debate caused tensions between The U. S and Mexico. After resolving many political
issues, a formal Treaty of Annexation was approved by the United States on December 29,
1845.""* On that day, the Republic of Texas became a state of the United States of America, The
State of Texas.

The aftermath of annexation was the Mexican — American War. Around 4 months after
annexation, the Mexican- American War began in April 1846." It was the first war in which the
United States fought on foreign soil. 1 am neither aware of documentation nor family lore that
any Grimes County Kennards participated in that war. It lasted until 1848. Mexico lost the war.
It lost to the U.S. almost one fourth of its existing territory, including all of present day
California, Utah, Nevada, Arizona, and New Mexico.*” The westward expansion of the U.S. was

given a significant boost.

Anthony Drew Kennard sent one of his sons (a boy at the time) from their Grimes
County residence to pick up supplies at a trading post in present day Madison County by way of
Old Springfield Road. Madison County is immediately north of Grimes County. The trading post
was about 15-20 miles from the Kennard home place “as the crow flies,” but considerably
farther using routes known at the time. The son rode a mare horse that had a foal which was
left at the Kennard home. Anyone with horse experience knows that all horses are always
anxious to return home from wherever they are. This is even more the case for a mare with a
young colt at home. She is lead by motherly instincts and the desire for the colt to nurse.

Once done with his business at the trading post, the boy felt like there must be shorter
way home than the route he took there. Having confidence that the instincts of the mare would
guide her back home the shortest way, the boy “gave the horse her head,” meaning that he did
not try to guide the horse home, but rather fet the horse find her own way back using her
instincts. According to EBR’s account, they encountered a hard rain drenching the young man
and his clothes. A group of friendly Indians of the Bedias Tribe took him in, dried his clothes,
and gave him food. The boy soon resumed his trip and continued to travel home with his horse
taking him through what is now Bedias and Singleton, Texas where the boy discovered a water

spring from which he drank.

32



--- PAGE BREAK ---

He then proceeded home through what is today Roans Prairie and on to the Kennard
home. Anthony Drew was surprised the boy returned so soon and was excited to learn of the
newly discovered water spring. The next morning, Anthony Drew got on his horse and followed
his son’s horse tracks left in the rain drenched mud to re-trace the shorter and quicker route to
the trading post and to the newly discovered spring. With a hand axe, he cut three notches in
trees along the way to mark a route for future use. Hence, the name “Three Notch Road.”

Not only the Kennards used this new trail to the trading post, but so did others including
Indians. Before long, the trail was used by horse, mule, and ox-drawn wagons and it became a
road that extended past what is today Bedias, Texas proceeding north toward Singleton and
Madisonville, Texas. It was the primary route between Anderson and Madisonville. Later the
new route was used by motor vehicles and eventually became paved roads maintained by
various governmental entities.

Today, part of the road is a highway with two lanes that is maintained by the State of
Texas, FM 2562. The highway runs past the original site of the Anthony Drew and Sarah
Kennard home place. They are both buried on that property next to the current fence line that
borders the right-of-way for that highway that some of the locals still call “Three Notch Read.”
It is used by passenger and commercial vehicles alike for all purposes. It all started one day with
a young boy’s decision in the 1830s to let his horse find its own way home.

Likewise, in 1936, a monument was erected at the east lawn of the Grimes County

Army during that war. Anthony Drew Kennard is prominently listed on that monument. I have

personally seen both sets of monuments and have pictures of them.

2. insight Into Mary Ann Womack Kennard.

EBR relates a story told to her by her grandmother Elizabeth Kennard Uzzell, a daughter

continued to attend to him, but he died 2 or 3 days later. People at that time presumed yellow
fever was highly contagious. Word spread in the community and surrounding area that the man
died of yellow fever. Rachael also became ill with yellow fever. People were genuinely
frightened of contracting the disease. Many even fled Anderson running from the yellow fever.
A doctor even ordered Rachael to be quarantined.

Somewhat amazingly, my great great grandmother, Mary Ann Womack Kennard,
bravely nursed her sister-in-law, Rachael, during her quarantine for 6 weeks. This was done in a
little log cabin, despite the risk to her own health and her own life and in face of the fear in the

community and surrounding area.**° Upon returning home every day from caring for Rachael,

--- PAGE BREAK ---

disease. Then, she would bathe in “strongly medicated water.”*””

the disease. This in no way, however, detracts from Mary Ann’s heroism. At the time of these

C. Children of Lizzie Kennard Hurst. ~ Lizzie and Jack Hurst had one son, Jack Hurst, Jr. He

and nerve-racking experiences of the Grimes County Kennards in World War Il. He was most
fortunate to have survived.

Jack Jr. told the story of his plane being shot down in enemy controlled territory and his

He told this story to his son, me, with his father, Uncle Jack present. We were in Aunt
Lizzie and Uncle Jack’s living room during the wintertime where there was a log fire burning in
the fireplace, our only source of heat for that room. This was only 14 or 15 years after these
events occurred. These memories were relatively fresh on his mind. He was reserved, but his
emotions were not entirely hidden because they were visible in his facial expressions. His
written version is most similar to his verbal rendition, but is written objectively and lacks the
emotions and facial expressions shown during his verbal rendition. Jack Jr. stared directly into
the fireplace fire and his eyes did not move during the entire story. Uncle Jack occasionally put
fresh logs on the fire during the story. A combined summary of the written and oral accounts
follows below.

Jack Jr. was a navigator for “Heavy Bomber” planes in the European Theatre. in England
on October 20, 1943, Jack Jr. was assigned to a B-17 aircraft. The mission of his squadron was to
bomb a rail junction in Duren, Germany connecting Cologne, Germany with Paris, France.
Germany occupied and controlled at the time. Five minutes away from the target in Duren,
Germany, the bomb bay doors were opened reducing the speed of the plane appreciably.

After experiencing anti-aircraft fire from the Germans that produced heavy flak, it
slacked off when the squadron approached the German target. The bombardier of his plane
ordered the bombs to be released over the target. After the bombs were released, all of the
bomb bay doors closed for the other planes in the squadron, “but alas, our doors did not close.”
While the other American planes proceeded back to base at faster speeds, the crew of Jack Jr.’s
plane had to manually close their doors. These events slowed them down appreciably. That left
them all alone and separated from the rest of the squadron, “not a comfortable feeling.”

Navigator, Jack Jr., directed the plane to fly back to their base by going between the large Dutch

cities of Rotterdam and Amsterdam. At that time, Holland (the Netherlands) where the Dutch
live, was also occupied and controlled by Germany.

Soon his plane was surrounded with Luftwaffe (German Air Force) fighter planes that
began firing on Jack Jr.’s plane while over the Netherlands. The attack upon them was severe.
During the attack, Jack Jr. noticed bullet holes in the fuselage. The bombardier who shared Jack
Jr.’s compartment in the plane then fell wounded. Two engines were on fire and 3
crewmembers had been killed. His plane was crashing. The remaining crew had to parachute
out of the plane in an effort to save their fives.

All airmen were trained to wait 60 seconds after jumping to open their parachutes to
avoid the parachute from getting tangled up in the plane. Jack Jr. helped the wounded
bombardier to the emergency exit. Following his best judgment under the circumstances, Jack
Jr, made an exception to what they were taught. He told the bombardier to pull his ripcord as
soon as he jumped in order to avoid passing out and being unable to do so. The bombardier did
so and his parachute successfully deployed. Jack Jr. then jumped out of the plane. Being
uninjured, he was able to follow his training by not pulling the ripcord for about 60 seconds.
This made his landing location far away from the other crew.

Three of the other crewmembers jumped together and were spotted by the German
planes. They were readily captured after landing. Jack Jr., on the other hand, landed about 1-%
miles away from them in a plowed field. Surely the soft plowed soil favorably affected his
landing which was safely done. However, a mean Jersey bull was near. They have a reputation
for being mean and aggressive. The bull charged but Jack Jr. was able to get out of the field
successfully. tt would have indeed ben ironic and tragic for him to survive the fighter plane
attack and the emergency parachute jump only to be injured (or worse) by a Jersey bull.

It would be 3 months of nerve racking and dangerous experiences before he returned to
England. He was reported as missing-in-action. One can only imagine the mental anguish his
parents, Aunt Lizzie and Uncle Jack, suffered during those 3 months.

Hitler’s Germany forcibly occupied the Netherlands during the war. Naturally, most

Dutchmen did not sympathize with the Germans. There was an active “Underground” in

Soon after landing, Jack Jr. met a sympathetic Dutchman who helped him out of his gear

Meanwhile, the Dutch Underground was trying to determine if Jack Jr. was a German
imposter. They asked him many questions. For example, what was served for breakfast at his
English base the morning they took off for the bombing run. Obviously the Underground was
informed by the Allies of the correct answer. Jack Jr. “passed” the test and was then informed
he would be put on a submarine to escape the country. At each point of this story, events could
result in his capture or death. In his verbal rendition, he stated he lived in constant fear of being
betrayed by one of the people “helping” him because of them being a secret German agent or
an opportunistic native of the area seeking favor from the Germans.

He spent a couple of weeks at Doorn, Netherlands before leaving to go south to meet
the submarine, but it never materialized. Instead, they rode bicycles to the border of Belgium,
which was also forcibly occupied and controlled by the German army. A simple waive at the
sentry inexplicably resulted in their crossing the border without incident. Had the sentry been
bribed or was he simply sympathetic? They proceeded by car to Brussels, Belgium where he
was kept in a house of ill repute. Then, a lady and her daughter took Jack Jr. to their home.
After a while, he proceeded to the crossing of the Belgian-French border. France was also
forcibly occupied by Germany at that time.

The Belgium Underground prepared a fake passport for him. To cross the border into
France, Jack Jr. played the role of a mute painter. They approached the crossing at 4:00 a.m.

when many factory workers moved back and forth each day. He saw two German guards. One

By this time, Jack Jr. was with other escapees some of which spoke different languages.
They forded a very cold river in the middle of December 1943. They were to cross the Pyrenees
Mountains to enter Spain. Spain’s military dictator at the time, Franco, was ideologically
sympathetic to Germany and Hitler. That was the major reason Germany never invaded Spain.
On the other hand, Brittan had provided much needed aid to Franco during the recent Spanish
Civil war. Truthfully, the Spanish people were deeply divided on where their loyalties should lie.
Franco never formally aligned with the Axis powers and continued to maintain a formal

Spain during the war.”

Jack Jr. states: “The trip through the Pyrenees (Mountains) was of short duration, only 8
days, but contained a lifetime of experiences.” They were 15 in number when they began,
travelling all night each night, and hiding out all day. Their guides for the trip through the
mountains were Catalons who were known to be unreliable, dishonest, and untrustworthy.

“Lower creatures never inhabited the earth. ... For a price these Catalons would do anything.”

The group traversed the mountainous countryside, crossing many vineyards, and never
using a road or pathway. Walking must have been most difficult. In addition, the Catalons beat
them on their backs with sticks, presumably to make them travel faster but possibly just for
meanness. They only ate food obtained from the countryside and traveled by night. One of the
travelers carried a valise that hampered his speed and prevented him from keeping up with the
rest of the group. He was repeatedly warned to keep up or else. Finally, one of the Catalone
guides, “placed his foot in the middle of the man’s rear end and booted him off the cliff’ to his
death! One can only imagine the impression this left upon Jack Jr. and his fellow escapees.

The feet of another person in the group became frostbitten causing him to turn-himself
-in at a monastery. He was captured by the Germans. That left 13 cold and weary escapees.

One morning they ran into a German patrol. They scattered “like a covey of quail.” The
German patrol acquired bloodhounds, and soon 10 of the remaining escapees were captured.
That teft only 3 escapees. The Germans were still after them with their dogs. The three were
exhausted and Jack Jr. took a Benzedrine pill for a needed surge of energy. They traveled a
mountain stream to cover their scent from the bloodhounds as well as their footprints. During
his verbal account, Jack Jr. told how they constantly heard the dogs barking while tracking
them. A slight inflexion of his voice at that point revealed the fear he experienced then.

Soon they were in Spain where the guides deserted them in a field of tall grass! They
were all alone in a completely foreign country where they did not speak the language. It was in
that field where they spent Christmas day, worried about their plight. Somehow, they ended up
in a friendly Spaniard’s home where they were given food and drink that was very much
needed. The next day, the three caught a train to Barcelona, Spain where they went to the
British Embassy. Amazingly, the Catalone guides were there too awaiting payment for their
“services.” At that point, it had been 30 days since Jack Jr. left Paris. He had not had a bath that

entire time. The three escapees went to a public bath, removed their clothes to never touch

From Barcelona, Spain they went to Madrid by car under the British Flag, the “Union

Embassy. On New Years Eve of 1943, he was supplied British stout beer, and he and two Indian
soldiers had quite a noisy New Years Eve. India at that time was a part of the British Empire.
From Madrid, Spain, a friendly boat captain allowed Jack Jr. to leave Spain by boat to Gibraltar,
an English Territory located at the southern tip of Spain. At last, he was totally free!

Jack Jr.’s ordeal was no doubt very much physically taxing in the extreme and
mentally stressful in the worst way. At any point, he could have easily been killed or captured
followed by torture and death. Missing from this story, however, is the emotional stress his
mother, Aunt Lizzie, and father, Uncle Jack, as well as the rest of the Grimes County Kennard
family and friends experienced during this time. We cannot forget that no one at home knew
whether Jack Jr.’s status. Was he dead, wounded, captured and suffering in a German prison, or
alive and in the dangerous process of escaping? The uncertainty must have been very hard to
cope with. Maintaining hope and avoiding depression had to be difficult for Aunt Lizzie and
Uncle Jack. You can bet that they shed tears during that time. My grandmother could only
shake her head back and forth while starring at the floor when describing her concern for her
sister, Aunt Lizzie, and brother-in-law, Uncle Jack, while Jack Jr. was missing in action. That does
not even address my grandmother's own personal concern over her nephew, Jack Jr., which
came through clearly when she talked about it. Everyone in Anderson and especially all of the
Grimes County Kennards were elated upon learning that Jack Jr. was unharmed, safe, healthy,
and free. The most grateful and happy, however, were his parents, Aunt Lizzie and Uncle Jack.

After the war, Jack Jr. stayed in regular contact with the Dutch family who took grave
risks helping him. He traveled to Holland a number of times and periodically sent them money
through the years. He and his wife, Mary, even named their two daughters after two of the
‘women in that family.

CONCLUSION

We are not necessarily special because of our ancestors, what they did, or where they
came from. Everyone’s ancestors came from somewhere and lived during the same eras as did
ours. To the contrary, we are what we are by virtue of the grace of God, and to a lesser degree,
our individual efforts and beliefs.

Ancestors are, nevertheless, our predecessors who provided a pathway for us. Family
heritage is an important part of how we got where we are and provides a partial explanation of
who we are. Knowledge of our family heritage is therefore important, but not required, to
understand who we are. Learning about our ancestors is, in my opinion, most worthwhile.

Further, we are allowed to be proud of what our ancestors did, the hardships they
endured, the risks that they took, and the sacrifices they made that inured to our benefit. The
Grimes County and Johnson County Kennards have a right to be proud that our ancestors
crossed the Atlantic Ocean when it was treacherous to do so. We have a right to be proud that
they lived in Maryland prior to the Revolutionary War against England at least as far back as
1650, and eventually left Maryland for North Carolina and Tennessee. We have a right to take
pride in our ancestors moving to Alabama, and becoming some of the initial Anglo-colonists
who settled Texas. In doing so, they took both personal and financial risks. They took extreme
risks in fighting indians. They “put it all on the line” by participating in the successful revolt
against the Mexican government resulting in Texas’ independence. Again, they risked
everything in the Civil War, but this time they lost. They financially pulled themselves back up

and again “put it all on the line for us” in World War fl. | do not know if our heritage is that
